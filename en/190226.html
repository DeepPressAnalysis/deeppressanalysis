IN FOCUS TODAY: US Predatory Hegemony, AI Agents Replacing Management, 'Centaurs' in the Labor Market, India Balancing Geopolitics, Trump vs. Latin America.

FOREIGN AFFAIRS

HEGEMONY • TRUMP • ASIA
The Trump administration in its second term has definitively shifted to a strategy of "predatory hegemony," abandoning the role of a global stabilizer. The hidden logic of Washington's actions lies in transforming international relations into a mechanism for direct rent extraction: allies and partners are viewed solely as resource sources obligated to pay for access to the US market and the "security umbrella." This creates an institutional risk for the existing system of alliances, as the US dismantles multilateral commitments in favor of bilateral deals where they hold an overwhelming advantage. For markets, this means rising geopolitical premiums and unpredictability in trade regimes, as tariffs are used as a weapon of political coercion. Globally, this pushes middle powers to seek strategic autonomy or alternative patrons (China), accelerating the fragmentation of the world.
The analysis reveals the fusion of US foreign policy with the interests of a transnational oligarchy. Trump and his inner circle use state instruments for personal enrichment and to support friendly kleptocratic regimes, ignoring traditional democratic values. The hidden threat lies in the "export of corruption": the US ceases to be a guarantor of transparency and the rule of law, turning into a hub for dirty money, which dilutes the standards of Western democracies from within. For business, this increases compliance risks and creates an uneven playing field where access to contracts depends on political loyalty rather than market efficiency.
The article deconstructs the liberal myth that supranational institutions are capable of solving modern challenges more effectively than nation-states. The logic of returning to sovereignty is dictated by the failure of global governance in areas of migration and climate; elites seek to regain control to avoid domestic destabilization. For corporations, this is a signal of impending regulatory de-harmonization: instead of uniform global rules, they will have to adapt to a mosaic of national requirements. Politically, this justifies the dismantling of structures like the UN and WTO in favor of direct "strong vs. strong" agreements, leaving small countries without the protection of international law.
The text refutes the popular narrative about the equality of poles in the new world, arguing that the US retains structural superiority but uses it more aggressively. The idea of multipolarity is beneficial to Washington as a pretext for relieving itself of responsibility for global public goods while retaining the right to unilateral action. For developing markets, this is a "trap": the expectation of greater room for maneuver is shattered by harsh coercion from the US to choose a side. Investors should prepare for American sanctions and extraterritorial law to be applied even more broadly and chaotically.
A scenario of strategic US retreat from the Indo-Pacific region and the collapse of the security guarantee system is modeled. The hidden logic points to the impossibility of the US simultaneously containing China and solving domestic problems, which will inevitably lead to a reduction of presence to the "second island chain." This creates an existential risk for allies (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan), pushing them toward nuclear armament and Finlandization. Economically, the region will be forced to reorient toward China, which will undermine dollar dominance and US technological standards in Asia.

THE ATLANTIC

FUTURE OF WORK • ISOLATIONISM • HEALTH CRISIS
The article analyzes the breaking of the social contract between labor and capital under the influence of AI. The benefits of automation will be privatized by algorithm owners, while the costs of unemployment will be nationalized, threatening a collapse in consumer demand. For corporations, this opens a path to radical OpEx reduction but creates a macroeconomic risk of a deflationary spiral. Politically, the situation is fraught with rising populism and demands for a universal basic income, which will increase the tax burden on business.
Robert Kagan describes the world's return to the state of 19th-century anarchy due to the US abandoning its role as "global policeman." Trump is intentionally dismantling the liberal order, viewing it as a burden, which legitimizes the right of might as the only international law. For global business, this means a sharp increase in security and insurance costs, as well as the end of the era of protected supply chains. Geopolitically, this unties the hands of regional aggressors and forces Europe and Asia to urgently militarize.
The public health crisis is viewed as a consequence of the disintegration of political institutions and trust, rather than just a medical problem. The slogan "Make America Healthy Again" is used to dismantle scientific expertise and federal agencies, paradoxically worsening the situation. For investors in pharma, this is a signal of high regulatory risks and rising anti-scientific sentiment affecting vaccine markets. Economically, the decline in the nation's health undermines labor productivity and US long-term competitiveness.
The ideologization of higher education is analyzed, where grant support (using the Mellon Foundation as an example) displaces classical knowledge in favor of social activism. The humanities are transforming from a tool of critical thinking into an instrument of political struggle, lowering the quality of human capital. For the labor market, this means an influx of personnel with strong ideological biases but weak analytical skills, creating risks for corporate culture and management.
Through the figure of a conservative intellectual, the drift of the American right toward anti-modernism and mysticism is shown. Culture wars escalate into a denial of Enlightenment foundations, aligning US conservatives with authoritarian regimes (Hungary). For the political system, this means compromise is impossible due to the rupture in the ontological realities of opponents. The risk for business is intensified ideological pressure on corporate policies (DEI, ESG) and the use of the state apparatus against "disloyal" companies.

AI BUSINESS MAGAZINE

AUTONOMOUS AGENTS • M&A • SOVEREIGN AI
A shift is recorded from generative AI to agentic AI: algorithms are beginning not just to create content, but to autonomously execute chains of business tasks. The hidden logic is a shift in the center of decision-making and the elimination of the middle management layer. For business, this promises explosive efficiency growth but creates risks of losing control over operational processes ("black box"). Software vendors are shifting to selling "digital workers," changing enterprise cost structures from OpEx to CapEx.
The death of the "per seat" payment model and the transition to an "outcome-based" model is analyzed. If AI replaces employees, the old licensing model collapses, threatening the revenues of traditional SaaS giants. The market awaits consolidation around platforms providing infrastructure for agents. For startups, the barrier to entry rises: now one must guarantee a business result, not just provide a tool.
AI radically accelerates M&A processes, allowing huge datasets to be analyzed in hours. The hidden risk is over-reliance on algorithms, which may lead to missing qualitative risks (culture, hidden conflicts). Strategically, this gives an advantage to large players ("predators") capable of instantly scanning the market and absorbing competitors, accelerating the monopolization of industries.
A trend toward the nationalization of AI infrastructure: states are building isolated "AI factories" to control data and security. This leads to the fragmentation of the global internet (splinternet) and growing demand for local data centers. For hardware suppliers, this is guaranteed government procurement, but for global cloud providers, it means a loss of markets in sensitive jurisdictions due to digital sovereignty requirements.
The use of AI in recruiting by small businesses levels the playing field in the war for talent, but the algorithmization of hiring cements systemic biases. The risk lies in personnel homogenization and the loss of the human factor at the entry point. For the labor market, this creates an industry of "optimization for bots," where filter-passing skills become more important than professional competencies.

TECH AI

AGI • CENTAURS • DATA CENTERS
The discussion about AGI is moving from science fiction to architectural solutions: from scaling models to hybrid systems (neural networks + symbolic computing). A hidden arms race: whoever reaches AGI first will gain military and economic dominance. For investors, this signals a potential change in market leaders if the current LLM approach hits a ceiling. Security risks and "control" (alignment) become the main technical barriers.
The concept of hybrid teams ("centaurs") as an intermediate stage before full automation. Winners are those who better manage AI agents, which devalues narrow hard skills. This deepens inequality in the labor market and requires restructuring corporate organizational charts. The hidden logic is preparing the ground for the gradual displacement of humans from the decision-making loop as agents improve.
The rising popularity of open-source and self-hosted solutions as a response to AI centralization in the hands of giants (OpenAI). Companies strive for independence and data privacy protection by deploying agents locally. This threatens the business model of API providers and stimulates the development of internal expertise. The risk is ecosystem fragmentation and security problems with custom-built solutions.
Rising energy needs for AI are leading to a symbiosis of big tech and the energy sector (including nuclear power). Data centers are becoming town-forming enterprises consuming regional resources, sparking environmental conflicts. For investors, this is a signal to look at cooling technologies and alternative energy.
The model race is turning into an ecosystem race. The model itself is becoming a commodity; value shifts to integration and data. The market is consolidating around giants capable of financing training and highly specialized players. For business, model selection becomes a strategic decision with high vendor lock-in risk.

FRONTLINE

INDIA • GEOPOLITICS • STATISTICS
The Indian view on Trump's policy: the end of strategic altruism and a shift to hard bargaining. India can no longer rely on the role of a "democratic counterweight" to China; loyalty to the US must now be bought with deals. Delhi is forced to balance (multi-alignment) between the US, Russia, and its own interests. The risk for the region is that the US stops investing in partners' institutions, demanding only the purchase of American goods and weapons.
A critique of economic statistics manipulation in India: the government hides real unemployment for the sake of ratings and investors. This creates an "expectation bubble" that threatens to burst when it collides with reality (consumption stagnation). Institutionally, trust in government data is being destroyed, turning the country's economy into a "black box" for analysis.
Actor Vijay's entry into politics reflects a global crisis of ideologies and a trend toward "savior" populism. The electorate is tired of old parties, preferring media personas without a clear program. The risk lies in the instability of such power and the erosion of party structures. This is a signal of the degradation of the political field to the level of a fan club, where emotions are more important than strategy.
Europe is losing moral authority and influence over the Global South due to double standards (supporting wars while preaching human rights). The hidden logic is that the EU is turning into a US vassal with no agency of its own. Economically, this threatens the loss of developing markets, which are reorienting toward China and Russia, perceiving Europe as an agent of neocolonialism.
The crisis of Sikh recruitment into the Indian army as a symptom of regional alienation and failed integration policy. The agrarian crisis and drug addiction are superimposed on political distrust of the center. The hidden risk is rising separatism and the marginalization of a border state, which creates a threat to national security and requires a revision of military personnel policy.

NEW INTERNATIONALIST

GLOBAL SOUTH • LATIN AMERICA • TECHNO-ETHICS
The piece attacks the narrative of AI "inevitability," viewing it as a Big Tech marketing strategy to bypass public consent and regulation. Hidden logic: corporations privatize productivity gains while offloading social and psychological costs (rising suicides, depression, loss of meaning) onto society. For investors, this is a signal of growing social resistance (techlash), which may result in strict regulatory barriers and consumer boycotts demanding a "human right" in service.
The return of the US to the "Monroe Doctrine" in its most aggressive form, including direct military actions (Maduro abduction), is analyzed. The hidden motive is not democracy, but strict control over oil resources (Venezuela, Guyana) and displacing China from the region. The risk lies in the total destabilization of the region and a shift to guerrilla warfare against US assets. Geopolitically, this finally buries Washington's "soft power," forcing Latin American countries to seek military protection from extra-regional players.
An investigation into the UAE's expansion in the Horn of Africa and Red Sea via port acquisitions and logistics infrastructure. The Emirates act as a sub-imperialist, projecting power and controlling key trade routes under the guise of commercial deals. This creates risks for global shipping, as commercial ports become dual-use bases. For global players, this signals the emergence of new ambitious power centers capable of redrawing influence maps in strategically vital regions on their own.
The article exposes the hidden human cost of "automation": AI functions only thanks to low-paid labor of moderators and data labelers in the Global South. The logic of digital neocolonialism: the mental health of peripheral workers is expended to ensure profits for centers (Silicon Valley). The risk for tech giants lies in ethical scandals, labor protests in supply chains, and the potential recognition of these workers as regular employees, which would destroy the economics of AI services.
A report on the movement to create the state of Bhil Pradesh in India illustrates the global trend of fragmenting large states. Indigenous peoples use identity as a tool to protect lands from corporate encroachment and resource extraction. For investors in the extractive sector, this means rising ESG risks and the possibility of projects being blocked locally. Politically, this is a symptom of the crisis of federalism, where the central government is perceived not as a guarantor of development, but as an agent of predatory capital.
