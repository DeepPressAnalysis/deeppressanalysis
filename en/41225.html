FINANCIAL TIMES

Fed policy, Russian assets, India, AI.
1
Investors warn Treasury against appointing Trump loyalist to head the Fed
▶
The bond market is sounding the alarm over a potential appointment of Kevin Hassett as chair of the Federal Reserve. Investors fear that a Fed chief loyal to Donald Trump could aggressively cut interest rates to serve White House politics, while downplaying inflation risks. That would undermine the central bank’s independence and could trigger volatility reminiscent of the UK gilt crisis in 2022. The episode highlights growing tension between traditional financial institutions and the new US administration.
2
EU plans to use Russian assets for Ukraine aid by bypassing vetoes
▶
Brussels has proposed a legal mechanism to use frozen Russian assets to back a loan to Ukraine worth up to €210 billion. The plan relies on emergency powers under Article 122 of the EU treaties, allowing decisions without unanimity and effectively neutralising Hungary’s veto. It would create a precedent for indefinitely freezing the Russian central bank’s assets until reparations are paid. The move is designed to secure long-term financial support for Kyiv despite legal risks for Europe’s financial system.
3
Putin and Modi deepen military and trade ties at Delhi summit
▶
Vladimir Putin’s visit to India underlines the resilience of the strategic partnership between Moscow and New Delhi, despite Western pressure and the threat of US secondary sanctions. The agenda includes fighter jet sales, air defence systems and expanded cooperation in nuclear energy. India continues to hedge, buying record volumes of Russian oil and weaponry, which is economically attractive but irritates Washington. For Russia, India remains a crucial channel for breaking isolation and maintaining export revenues.
4
AI developer Anthropic prepares major IPO, racing ahead of OpenAI
▶
Anthropic, the company behind the Claude chatbot, has hired a law firm to prepare for an initial public offering, aiming to reach public markets before its main rival OpenAI. Recent private funding rounds value the company at more than $40 billion, underscoring the ongoing frenzy around generative artificial intelligence. The listing will be a test of public investors’ appetite for loss-making but fast-growing AI labs. A successful IPO would cement Anthropic’s position as one of the leaders in the global AI race.
5
Israel faces political crisis over conscription of ultra-Orthodox Jews
▶
Prime minister Benjamin Netanyahu is confronting the risk of coalition collapse over a bill regulating the draft of ultra-Orthodox Jews into the army. Amid a prolonged war in Gaza and a shortage of personnel for the IDF, public opinion is demanding an end to historic exemptions for the Haredim, provoking fierce resistance from religious parties — key pillars of the government. The standoff exposes a deep social rift and threatens political stability in wartime. A sustainable compromise looks out of reach, increasing domestic turbulence.
6
EU agrees full ban on Russian gas imports by 2027
▶
The European Union has reached a political agreement to phase out all Russian gas imports, including LNG, by autumn 2027. The decision, taken against the backdrop of the ongoing war in Ukraine, includes temporary exemptions for vulnerable countries such as Hungary and Slovakia in case of supply shortages. The move locks in Europe’s shift towards US and Norwegian suppliers. For Russia, it means the loss of its largest historic export market with little chance of a quick replacement.

DAILY MAIL

Meghan Markle’s father, digital surveillance, Labour rebels and royal diplomacy.
1
Thomas Markle in intensive care as Duchess’s father fights for his life after surgery
▶
News that 81-year-old Thomas Markle is in critical condition in a Philippine hospital once again revives the story of his estrangement from daughter Meghan. The situation creates intense media pressure on the Sussexes, pushing them towards a public response or a gesture of reconciliation. It underlines a continuing personal crisis within the royal family that tabloids can revisit whenever there is fresh drama around the couple.
2
Labour government plans mass rollout of facial recognition cameras
▶
Keir Starmer’s government is preparing to deploy facial recognition technology across all police forces — a step critics describe as turning Britain into a “Big Brother state”. The system would allow police to match CCTV images against not only their own databases but also passport photos of ordinary citizens. The move marks a shift by Labour towards a hard law-and-order agenda, despite serious concerns over civil liberties and surveillance.
3
Rural Labour MPs plot revolt against Reeves over “farm tax”
▶
Internal tensions inside Labour are escalating as MPs from rural constituencies fear losing their seats over an unpopular inheritance tax on farms. A move to oust chancellor Rachel Reeves shows how fragile party discipline becomes once economic discontent among voters rises. The dispute threatens to damage the government’s authority on fiscal policy and undermine its promise of economic stability.
4
Paedophile at elite nursery pleads guilty to filming abuse of children
▶
Vincent Chan, an employee at the high-end Bright Horizons nursery, has admitted to systematic abuse of children and creating around 25 000 prohibited images. The case exposes serious failures in staff vetting procedures even in the premium segment of childcare services. It is likely to trigger a wave of inspections across nurseries and tougher requirements for safeguarding and background checks.
5
Kate Middleton wears rare tiara for state banquet honouring German president
▶
The Princess of Wales’s decision to wear a tiara not seen in public since the 1930s at the banquet for president Steinmeier is a carefully calibrated piece of royal soft power. The visit is aimed at rebuilding post-Brexit ties with a key EU partner. The royal family continues to act as one of Britain’s most effective diplomatic tools abroad, projecting continuity amid political instability at home.
6
Jenni Murray links foot paralysis to weight-loss injections
▶
Veteran broadcaster Jenni Murray has publicly linked neurological problems in her feet to the use of fashionable weight-loss injections. Her story feeds into a growing debate about the side effects of blockbuster anti-obesity drugs. It may shift public perceptions of how safe these treatments really are and add pressure for further medical studies and tighter regulation.

THE GUARDIAN

NHS crisis, Russian assets for Ukraine, trans rights and right-wing manoeuvres.
1
Leaked report exposes bullying culture and patient risk at Blackpool hospital
▶
A confidential Royal College of Physicians report confirms systemic bullying and racism at Blackpool Victoria hospital, directly undermining patient safety. The scandal reveals a deep management crisis inside the NHS and the failure of leadership to protect both staff and patients. It piles pressure on health secretary Wes Streeting to deliver meaningful reform rather than rhetoric on “modernising” the service.
2
Brussels proposes using frozen Russian assets to fund loans to Kyiv
▶
The European Commission has drafted a mechanism to extend loans to Ukraine backed by profits from frozen Russian state assets, circumventing vetoes from individual member states. The plan is an attempt to secure long-term financing for Kyiv amid uncertainty over US aid. It establishes a legal precedent for using sovereign assets of a sanctioned state as collateral in a geopolitical conflict.
3
Nigel Farage plots “reverse takeover” of the Conservative Party
▶
Reform UK leader Nigel Farage has openly stated that he aims to absorb the Conservative base and party machine, rather than enter into an alliance. This signals a long-term strategy to reshape the British right along populist lines. For the weakened Conservatives, the threat is existential: they risk being reduced to a hollow shell if they cannot rebuild credibility with their core voters.
4
Whistleblower claims Foreign Office deleted “genocide” warnings on Sudan
▶
Foreign Office staff allegedly removed the word “genocide” from internal briefing notes on Sudan to avoid upsetting the UAE, which backs one side in the conflict. The allegation strikes at the moral authority of British diplomacy and suggests geopolitical interests are being placed above human rights. The scandal may strain relations with NGOs and damage the UK’s image as a defender of international law.
5
Women’s Institute bans trans women from membership
▶
Britain’s largest women’s organisation, the WI, has changed its constitution to restrict membership to biological women, citing legal exposure and safeguarding duties. The decision reflects a shift in public debate over gender, single-sex spaces and competing rights. It is a clear win for “gender-critical” feminism and a setback for the inclusivity agenda pushed by trans-rights activists.
6
Health department to probe surge in ADHD and autism diagnoses
▶
Health secretary Wes Streeting has ordered an investigation into the sharp rise in diagnoses of neurodivergent conditions and related disability benefit claims. The government is worried about the growing fiscal burden on the welfare system and the risk of over-diagnosing what may be normal variations in behaviour. The review could lead to stricter eligibility criteria and a political backlash from disability campaigners.

THE DAILY TELEGRAPH

State surveillance, Chinese influence in schools, A&E crisis and budget leaks.
1
Police to gain access to passport database for facial recognition system
▶
The Home Office plans to give police access to the passport photo database of all 45 million UK citizens for use with live facial recognition. This is a fundamental step towards pervasive biometric surveillance, eroding privacy for law-abiding people as much as for criminals. Civil liberties groups are sharply critical, but ministers frame the move as a breakthrough tool in the fight against crime and disorder.
2
Mandarin programme teaches children that Taiwan is part of China
▶
Teaching materials used in the government-backed Mandarin Excellence Programme present Taiwan as part of the PRC, mirroring Beijing’s political line. The revelation shows how Chinese soft power can seep into the UK education system through language and cultural initiatives. The row will fuel demands to reassess partnerships with institutions linked to the Chinese state and to tighten scrutiny of classroom content.
3
A&E departments overwhelmed by patients with hiccups and colds
▶
New data show accident-and-emergency departments are being flooded with patients seeking treatment for trivial ailments such as hiccups and mild colds. This highlights the collapse of primary care: with GP access constrained, people head straight to hospital. Ministers want pharmacies to take on a greater role in front-line care to ease pressure on the NHS ahead of winter, but that will not fix deeper structural problems in the system.
4
Labour MP suspended for voting against “farm tax” inheritance plans
▶
Labour backbencher Marcus Campbell-Savours has been stripped of the whip after being the only party MP to vote against farm inheritance tax proposals. The harsh response shows how nervous the leadership is about rural anger over the policy. It is also a warning shot to other potential rebels that dissent on core economic measures will not be tolerated in Keir Starmer’s party.
5
Chancellor Reeves launches probe into budget leaks to the press
▶
Chancellor Rachel Reeves has ordered an internal investigation to identify the source of leaks that unsettled markets ahead of the Budget. The move is an attempt to restore discipline inside the Treasury and shift blame for the negative market reaction onto alleged “saboteurs”. The episode undermines confidence in the new economic team’s grip on communications and market expectations management.
6
Foreign Office found at fault over delay in response to Harry Dunn’s death
▶
An independent report has concluded that the Foreign Office was too slow to act in the case of teenager Harry Dunn, killed in a crash involving the wife of a US diplomat. The findings confirm that maintaining smooth relations with Washington took precedence over securing justice for a British citizen. The report calls for a rethink of how diplomatic immunity is handled when serious crimes and public outrage are involved.

DAILY EXPRESS

Business taxes, war with doctors’ unions, royal visit and victims’ rights.
1
Kemi Badenoch accuses Starmer of “destroying” small business with tax hikes
▶
Conservative heavyweight Kemi Badenoch is attacking the government over higher business rates for the hospitality sector, branding Labour’s approach a “mission to wipe out the high street”. The opposition is trying to re-position itself as the champion of small business and entrepreneurs, tapping into anger over the Budget. This is a central Conservative line of attack as they seek to rebuild economic credibility with their traditional voter base.
2
Health secretary calls striking doctors “whingers” and “juvenile delinquents”
▶
Wes Streeting has dramatically escalated his rhetoric against the British Medical Association, refusing to give ground on pay demands and labelling some striking medics “whingers” and “juvenile delinquents”. Such aggressive language from a Labour minister is highly unusual and is meant to signal that the government will not be held hostage by unions. The gamble could either break the strike or leave the NHS paralysed heading into winter.
3
Police expand use of facial recognition despite privacy concerns
▶
Ministers are pushing ahead with rolling out facial recognition technology, downplaying the objections of civil liberties groups. The Express frames this as a tough but necessary response to public demands for law and order. The clash over surveillance tools has become a cultural dividing line between liberal urban opinion and a more conservative electorate that prioritises safety over abstract privacy arguments.
4
New laws to stop defence lawyers using victims’ sexual history in rape trials
▶
The government is introducing reforms to bar defence lawyers from weaponising a rape victim’s sexual history in court. The changes are aimed at raising conviction rates in sexual offence cases by protecting complainants from humiliating cross-examination. The move responds to long-standing criticism of the justice system’s failure to deliver outcomes in rape cases, though some legal experts warn it could restrict the right to a full defence.
5
Princess of Wales steals the show at banquet with German president
▶
The paper focuses on the glamour of the state banquet, casting the Princess of Wales as the star of the event. Her visibility reinforces the monarchy’s role as a symbol of continuity and stability during political upheaval. The successful visit is also important for maintaining post-Brexit links with Europe’s largest economy, with the royal family serving as a key asset in UK soft-power diplomacy.
6
Charities report record demand for food among pensioners
▶
A surge in older people turning to food banks points to a deepening cost-of-living crisis among pensioners. For the Express readership, stories of elderly citizens having to seek charity for basic nutrition are politically explosive. The trend is a direct reproach to the government, which has cut winter fuel payments, and makes poverty in old age one of the most sensitive issues in the current economic debate.
