Strategic consequences and hidden threats: Analysis of cascading risks in a 1 to 3-month horizon

Analysis of the global dataset for May 2026 reveals an unprecedented synchronization of crises across geopolitics, macroeconomics, energy, and technology. The current status quo has been disrupted by fundamental shifts generating critical systemic pressure. Based on the evaluation of hidden incentives, real resource flows, and asymmetric effects, four key macro-trends with the highest potential for short-term cascading destruction have been highlighted. Events no longer unfold in isolation: the energy blockade overlaps with the institutional crisis in the US, which in turn accelerates the technological fragmentation of the world and heightens the risks of deploying autonomous intelligent systems.

01

Military blockade of the Strait of Hormuz and the "ceasefire mirage" phenomenon

The US military operation Epic Fury, which lasted 38 days and included over 10,200 combat sorties and 13,000 strikes on Iran's infrastructure, is officially presented as an unquestionable success. Specifically, claims are made regarding the destruction of 90% of Iran's naval force and drone production base. However, the physical reality of global logistics dictates entirely different conditions: the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively blocked, creating an unprecedented state that analysts describe as a "ceasefire with no oil purgatory." This gap between military rhetoric and economic realities generates colossal systemic pressure.

Primary effect

The direct consequence of the current situation has been the removal of approximately 15% of the global oil supply from the global logistics chain. Even though Iranian military aviation has been neutralized (daily sorties reduced from 100 to zero), asymmetric control over the strait remains due to the threat of mines and residual capabilities. The basic security architecture in the Middle East has drastically changed: the United Arab Emirates and Saudi Arabia directly participated in military operations for the first time, launching airstrikes against Iran using their own air forces and Chinese-made drones, thereby definitively destroying the old status quo of proxy wars. Brent crude is trading steadily in a range near $100 per barrel, occasionally dipping to $98 solely on unfounded hopes of de-escalation.

Second-order effects

In the coming weeks, the world will face a severe physical shortage of petroleum products that cannot be mitigated through monetary methods. The Asian region has already taken the first hit, reflected in physical consumption restrictions: Bangladesh has introduced limits on indoor cooling, Laos has reduced the school week to three days, and Sri Lanka has declared Wednesdays a national holiday to save fuel. Europe faces a critical shortage of aviation kerosene: according to International Energy Agency estimates, reserves will last approximately six weeks, which will inevitably lead to massive commercial flight cancellations and the paralysis of air logistics. In Japan, Sanae Takaichi's government is forced to tap into 1 trillion yen in emergency reserve funds to subsidize gas and electricity prices ahead of peak summer demand.

Third-order effects and pressure migration

On a systemic level, there is a destruction of trust in American security guarantees. The conflict has clearly demonstrated that absolute conventional superiority cannot guarantee the continuity of commercial shipping in narrow straits when the adversary possesses asymmetric deterrence capabilities. Systemic pressure is rapidly migrating from the purely military sphere to civilian logistics and state fiscal policy. The impact shifts to developing energy-importing countries (South Asia), then to the European transport sector, and ultimately to the financial systems of developed nations (Japan, EU), which will be forced to increase their sovereign debt burden to subsidize energy resources for their populations and prevent social unrest.

Hidden winners and losers

The hidden beneficiaries are countries possessing hydrocarbon reserves outside the conflict zone, primarily Brazil and Guyana. China gains a massive hidden advantage: possessing large-scale strategic reserves and access to discounted Russian oil, Beijing not only ensures internal stability but also resells excess oil at inflated prices on the open market, reaping significant arbitrage profits. The losers are not only airlines and the poorest Asian countries; complex high-tech manufacturing chains in Europe suffer massive hidden losses due to their critical dependence on stable electricity prices. The US consumer also loses, with their purchasing power methodically destroyed by rising gasoline prices surpassing $4.50 per gallon.

02

Institutional erosion and subordination of the US monetary-statistical apparatus

In May 2026, the US Senate confirmed Kevin Warsh as Chairman of the Federal Reserve by an unprecedentedly narrow and deeply partisan majority (54-45). This decision was made amid open and aggressive pressure from President Donald Trump, who demands a drastic reduction in interest rates to stimulate the economy, despite sticky consumer inflation stalled at 3.8% and 10-year Treasury yields hovering around 4.5%. The situation is critically exacerbated by the prior dismissal of the Director of the Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS), Erica McEntaffer, in August 2025, under the baseless pretext of "falsifying employment data."

Primary effect and second-order risks

There has been a direct and unprecedented violation of the institutional independence of key US economic and statistical departments. Markets perceive Warsh's appointment not as a monetary paradigm shift, but as a political capitulation by the Fed to the White House. Warsh has already openly declared his intention to abandon the practice of publishing detailed macroeconomic forecasts (dot plots) and to "reduce communications," so as not to become "a hostage to their own words." In the coming months, global financial markets will face extreme and unpredictable volatility. The Fed's abandonment of transparent communication forces institutional investors to operate in informational blindness. Algorithmic trading systems used by market makers will increasingly pivot toward alternative private-sector data, which exhibits much higher volatility.

Third-order effects and capital flows

A structural, latent, yet irreversible destruction of trust in the US dollar as the world's primary risk-free asset is underway. If sovereign investors can no longer trust American statistics, they will logically demand compensation in the form of higher yields on Treasury bonds (term premium). This will drive up the cost of servicing the astronomical US public debt. There is a systemic flight of "smart capital" away from long-term Treasuries. Capital is rapidly flowing into inflation-protected niche instruments, private credit funds, and shares of mega-cap tech corporations, which, amid macroeconomic chaos, are viewed as quasi-sovereign safe havens.

03

Asymmetric technological repatriation amid the US-China diplomatic stalemate

The May 2026 summit in Beijing between Presidents Donald Trump and Xi Jinping concluded in a profoundly paradoxical manner: public diplomatic rhetoric about "strategic stability" and avoiding the "Thucydides trap" completely contrasts with the reality of zero trade breakthroughs and an ongoing, harsh technological war. In the shadow of these empty declarations, China has launched and scaled an unprecedented program to repatriate tech talent (known as "sea turtles") from Silicon Valley.

Primary effect and second-order risks

The direct consequence of the current status quo is the strategic failure of US attempts to limit China's technological sovereignty by relying exclusively on hardware export controls. Beijing has realized that capital and "hardware" are secondary to intellectual capital. Using massive targeted state subsidies (reaching $14.7 million per project in Shanghai), the Chinese government is actively and successfully luring key engineers, AI researchers, and senior executives directly from American corporations (Meta, Google, Amazon, Anthropic). In the short term, the massive exodus of highly skilled Chinese-origin specialists from Silicon Valley will create a tangible skill shortage in the most complex development nodes of generative and agentic AI, as well as quantum computing.

Structural and temporal shifts

This trend is deeply structural. According to LinkedIn data, 59% of Chinese students pursuing their PhDs abroad plan to return to China (up from 38% the previous year). The historic "Brain Drain" from Asia to the US is completely reversing direction; this is a tectonic shift that will alter the global innovation balance for decades to come. The hidden beneficiaries are European and Japanese manufacturers of specialized semiconductor equipment (STMicroelectronics, Tokyo Electron), whose stocks are experiencing rapid growth as global capital desperately seeks entry points into the AI industry outside the direct US-China geopolitical conflict zone.

04

Transformation of the decision-making architecture: Transition to Agentic AI under excessive capital concentration

The evolution of artificial intelligence has rapidly moved past the simple content generation stage (GenAI) and advanced toward agentic systems (Agentic AI), capable of autonomously structuring logic and executing complex, multi-component business processes. This is occurring against a backdrop of unprecedented and historically anomalous capital concentration in a narrow tech sector. Simultaneously, a massive AI infrastructure is consuming volumes of electricity comparable to the consumption of entire countries.

Primary effect and "AI fatigue"

The technological paradigm of labor automation is radically shifting. Agentic AI does not merely generate text on demand; it makes management decisions applying probabilistic reasoning. However, these systems are inherently prone to unpredictable errors ("hallucinations"), which no longer translate into flawed text on a screen, but directly into business decisions and transactions. Evidence of this danger is the recent scandal involving the Canadian branch of the consulting giant Ernst & Young (EY): a comprehensive study on the loyalty program market, generated by AI and sold to clients, contained completely fabricated data and nonexistent margins, dealing a severe blow to the firm's reputation. This is generating a massive phenomenon known as "AI Fatigue" among middle management.

Narrative manipulation and losers

The C-suite of tech giants deliberately feeds the global media narrative regarding the "existential threat" that AI poses to humanity. The true objective is to create an insurmountable regulatory moat that will require costly licensing, permanently closing the market to smaller competitors and open-source solutions. The main hidden losers are the "Big Four" consultancies and elite law firms. Their core business model, centered on primary information gathering and analysis, is being automated at extraordinary speed, bringing colossal reputational risks from uncontrolled AI use (EY and Sullivan & Cromwell scandals). Business Process Outsourcing (BPO) firms also face significant losses, as their business model, built on cheap human labor, is being demolished by Agentic AI workflows.

Executive Summary and Final Synthesis

The analysis highlights a critical, synchronous convergence of crises. Geopolitical and energy inflation overlaps with deliberate institutional dysfunction in the US, global tech balkanization, and dangerous shifts in corporate risk management structure. Systemic stability is currently sustained solely by artificial political narratives, while physical resource flows point inevitably to imminent cascading failures.

The four most probable points of systemic tension (1 to 3-month horizon):

  • UK government crisis and pound sterling shock: The collapse of Keir Starmer's government under union pressure and Andy Burnham's by-election victory will trigger foreign capital flight and a plunge in the pound.
  • Physical collapse of European aviation logistics: Depletion of the six-week aviation kerosene reserves due to the Middle East blockade will cause chaotic cancellations of commercial and passenger flights.
  • Algorithmic "Flash Crash" in the US bond market: Sudden liquidity failure during a Treasury auction. Due to lost trust in BLS data, algorithms will preemptively pull liquidity, causing a sudden yield spike.
  • Corporate AI Liability Shock: An autonomous agentic AI system in a major investment fund or corporation, acting on a probabilistic hallucination, will execute a series of irrevocable erroneous transactions, resulting in a market cap collapse and a loss of trust in enterprise (B2B) AI systems.
