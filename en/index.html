01

How to Invest Like Warren Buffett: The End of an Era and the Start of Uncertainty

▶
Warren Buffett's departure yesterday (December 31, 2025) has officially stripped Berkshire Hathaway of its "safe haven" status, converting the conglomerate into a standard, albeit overcapitalized, holding company. The market enters 2026 with a new reality: the "Buffett Premium"—that irrational stock valuation buffer based on faith in one man's genius—has evaporated, exposing real operational risks. For institutional investors, this is the signal to begin an aggressive revaluation of assets using the "sum-of-the-parts" method, which will inevitably lead to volatility and pressure on the stock. Greg Abel takes office under conditions where any misstep will be interpreted not as a management error, but as a systemic collapse of the post-Buffett model. This creates ideal conditions for hedge funds and short-sellers ready to attack the giant.
02

Anthropic Is Still Hung Up on 'AI Safety.' Turns Out, Big Business Loves That

▶
Anthropic is cynically monetizing corporate paranoia by turning the abstract concept of "safety" into a high-margin B2B product. While competitors chase raw compute power, the company is selling "insurance" against reputation lawsuits to big business, effectively taxing boardroom fear regarding the unpredictability of neural networks. This creates a dangerous precedent of market segmentation: access to "safe" AI becomes a privilege for the Fortune 500, while the mass market is left with less controlled and potentially hazardous versions. Anthropic's strategy isn't about ethics; it's about creating a gated community for corporate giants.
03

Nvidia Is Invincible. Unless It Isn't

▶
Nvidia's current position is a textbook example of "capital cannibalization" within a closed ecosystem. Revenue growth is driven not by end-consumer demand, but by the panic-induced Capex of hyperscalers hoarding chips to secure market share in an economy that doesn't exist yet. This creates a liquidity bubble: money is transferring from Microsoft and Google shareholders' pockets into Jensen Huang's pocket without generating real surplus value. If Q1 2026 earnings from big tech don't show explosive profit growth from AI implementation, the market faces a brutal correction.
04

How Rare Earths Became Ground Zero in the U.S.-China Rivalry

▶
Washington's intervention in MP Materials marks the final death of the free market in strategic sectors and the transition to a "military state capitalism" model. Investors must understand: any assets linked to defense and chip supply chains are now hostages to geopolitics, where profitability is secondary to national security. China, controlling processing, has its hand on the switch of global tech inflation; any export restriction would instantly crush the margins of Western electronics manufacturers.
05

How an AI Bubble Could Ruin the Party

▶
The disparity between infrastructure investment ($400 billion) and actual revenue ($13 billion) isn't just a gap; it's a financial abyss threatening the stability of the entire banking system. The market is overheated with expectations that mathematically cannot be realized within priced-in timeframes; we are entering the disillusionment phase, which will hit passive investors' wallets through falling indices. If infrastructure doesn't start paying for itself in 2026, a cascade of write-offs will begin, comparable to the dot-com crash, only with higher leverage.

All Revved Up: Corvette Stingray

▶
The expansion of gas-guzzling V8s into the Singapore market in 2026 is not a triumph of engineering, but a cynical cash grab by General Motors to squeeze the last drop of profit from dying technology in regions with excess liquidity. By selling morally obsolete ICE vehicles as elite exotics, the conglomerate is parasitizing the nostalgia of wealthy Asians while ignoring global climate trends. 02

The Commission Of The Century: Rolls-Royce Phantom

▶
The release of the centenary Phantom confirms Rolls-Royce's strategy of turning automobiles into instruments of social segregation. The move to total exclusivity (Private Office) is a response to the dilution of the "premium" concept: when too many people have a Mercedes, a Rolls-Royce must be unattainable even for the merely rich. The brand creates artificial scarcity, turning a vehicle into an investment token. Owning such an asset becomes not a question of taste, but a question of access to a closed resource distribution club.
03

La Fenomeno: Tecnomar for Lamborghini 101FT

▶
Lamborghini's collaboration with a shipyard is aggressive logo monetization bordering on brand dilution for quick cash. Automakers have hit a sales ceiling "on wheels" and are now trying to sell air—"lifestyle"—to the same clients whose garages are already full. The buyer overpays millions exclusively for the badge, receiving a product with dubious residual value in the secondary yacht market, where specialized maritime brands are valued.
04

Cabin Couture Takes Off

▶
The trend for radical customization of private jet interiors demonstrates a dangerous decoupling of the ultra-rich from economic reality. Investing millions in interiors that are unsellable (due to specific owner tastes) shows that private aviation is no longer viewed as a rational business tool. It is pure capital incineration to satisfy ego, rendering the asset toxic for the secondary market.
05

Adrenaline Pill Hammers Down...

▶
The sale of a sculpture for €27,600 is a glaring indicator of the overheating alternative investment market and the triumph of marketing over meaning. Auction houses are legitimizing blatant kitsch, blowing bubbles around objects with no intrinsic artistic value to attract "new money" liquidity. This is the "Greater Fool Theory" in action: the asset is bought solely with the hope of selling it at a higher price to the next victim of the hype cycle.
