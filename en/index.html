DECEMBER 30, 2025 Christmas Edition
Holiday Digest: The Market Bubble, Trump & Zelensky at the Vatican, Cheap Chinese AI, and the US Semiquincentennial.

BLOOMBERG BUSINESSWEEK

Market Bubble • Rare Earths • Oracle AI • Trump Crypto • Retail
01

Beware The Bubble: How to survive the year ahead

▼
Markets are entering a phase of "irrational exuberance" reminiscent of the eve of the 1929 crash or the Dot-com bubble. The primary risk for investors lies in the synchronized inflation of bubbles across all asset classes: from tech stocks and real estate to cryptocurrencies and private credit. The AI sector is of particular concern, where capital expenditures on infrastructure (data centers) significantly outpace actual monetization of the technology. The deflation of this bubble could trigger a cascading liquidity effect, as many projects are financed through opaque debt and "shadow banking." The Federal Reserve finds itself trapped: fighting inflation requires high rates, but this threatens to collapse overheated markets. For strategic planning, it is crucial to recognize that current growth is driven not by fundamentals, but by speculative capital and FOMO.
02

America's rare-earth mining pipe dream

▼
US attempts to create a rare-earth supply chain independent of China are colliding with the harsh reality of Chinese monopoly. Beijing controls not only extraction but, more importantly, processing technologies, using export restrictions as a geopolitical weapon. For the American defense industrial base and the green energy sector, this creates critical risks of long-term component shortages. Investments in alternative technologies, such as iron nitride magnets, remain in early stages and cannot replace Chinese imports in the coming years. The logic of a trade war works against Washington here: China can afford short-term losses to maintain its strategic leverage. Tech investors must factor in the risk of sudden raw material supply disruptions.
03

Oracle has a lot riding on AI infrastructure

▼
Larry Ellison is pivoting the database giant toward building massive data centers for AI training, entering direct competition with traditional cloud providers. The strategy involves aggressive debt financing and partnerships with key players like OpenAI, carrying high corporate risk. If demand for generative AI falls below expectations, or if key clients (startups) fail to reach profitability, Oracle will be left with massive illiquid assets. Conversely, success could transform the company into a primary beneficiary of the "arms race" for computing power. This signals a shift in competition from model development to physical infrastructure and energy access.
04

Trump forged a memecoin fortune

▼
The integration of politics and unregulated crypto assets is reaching a new level, creating a precedent for direct conflict of interest at the highest level of government. The launch of politically affiliated tokens signals the conversion of electoral support into a speculative financial instrument. This creates risks for retail investors, as asset volatility depends not on market mechanics but on political statements and polling numbers. For regulators, this is a "gray zone" that may lead to weakened oversight of the crypto market as a whole under administrative pressure. Geopolitically, this undermines trust in the dollar as a neutral asset, pushing investors toward alternative safe havens.
05

Can new leadership make Target chic again?

▼
The retail giant is attempting to adapt to a bifurcation in consumer demand, where shoppers simultaneously demand low prices and a premium experience. The strategy of moving away from simple competition with Walmart and Amazon toward creating unique private labels and collaborations carries operational risks. Success depends on the accuracy of AI-driven trend forecasting and supply chain agility. For the market, this is a stress test for the middle class: if the strategy fails, it will indicate declining purchasing power in the core of the US economy. Investors should watch retailer margins as a leading indicator of consumer confidence.

PICTURES OF THE YEAR 2025

Trump/Zelensky • Tariff Wars • Vatican • Waymo Luddism • Climate
01

One-On-One: U.S. President Donald Trump and Ukraine's President Volodymyr Zelensky

▼
The leaders' meeting at the Vatican, against the backdrop of the Pope's funeral, symbolizes a paradigm shift in international diplomacy. The transition to a transactional relationship format, characteristic of the Trump administration, threatens established alliances and security guarantees. For Ukraine, this signals the need to find new leverage and potentially adjust its negotiating position. European partners view this as a risk of reduced American engagement in continental security, pushing the EU toward strategic autonomy. Markets react to such meetings with increased volatility in the defense sector and emerging market currencies.
02

Tariffs Hit the Breaks

▼
The imposition of harsh import tariffs (including 25% on autos and up to 145% on certain goods from China) is triggering a structural realignment of global trade. Photos of overflowing ports and jewelry stores where people are selling gold at peak prices illustrate the inflationary consequences of protectionism. For business, this means rising costs and the urgent need to diversify supply chains bypassing the US and China. Beneficiaries include intermediary nations (Vietnam, Mexico), while the end consumer in the US faces reduced goods availability. Long-term, this leads to the fragmentation of the global economy into isolated trade blocs.
03

Farewell to Francis

▼
The death of Pope Francis and the subsequent conclave open a period of uncertainty for the Vatican as a key player in "soft power." Francis was a figure uniting the Global South and Western liberal values; his departure may strengthen the conservative wing of the church. This has geopolitical significance, as the Vatican often acts as an intermediary in back-channel diplomacy. The struggle for the Throne of St. Peter will reflect the global confrontation between progressivism and traditionalism. For institutional investors, this is a factor of social instability in Catholic nations across Latin America and Europe.
04

Up In Smoke: A Waymo car burns during protests

▼
The destruction of a Waymo robotaxi by protesters in Los Angeles marks the beginning of a new phase of Luddism—active resistance to the integration of AI into the physical world. Social tension caused by fear of job loss and algorithmic control is turning into violence. For tech companies, this presents a serious regulatory and reputational risk that could stall the deployment of autonomous transport. Insurers will be forced to reassess risk models for robotic assets. Politically, this creates fertile ground for populist movements demanding technology restrictions to protect labor.
05

Feeling the Heat / California Burning

▼
A series of massive wildfires in Spain and California, coinciding with record temperatures, confirms the failure of current climate adaptation strategies. The destruction of infrastructure and housing stock is leading to a crisis in the reinsurance market: some regions are becoming economically uninhabitable due to the inability to insure property. This creates long-term pressure on state budgets forced to absorb reconstruction costs. For the agricultural sector and tourism (especially in Southern Europe), this is an existential threat requiring a radical shift in business models.

TIME BEST INVENTIONS 2025

Chinese AI • Satellite Internet • Quantum • Deepfakes • Lab Meat
01

DeepSeek R1: A low-cost LLM threatening Big Tech

▼
The emergence of the Chinese model DeepSeek R1, trained for just $6 million, undermines the economic model of Western AI giants. If advanced models can be created for a fraction of the billion-dollar budgets of OpenAI or Google, the "era of massive investment" may end in the commoditization of technology. This lowers entry barriers for startups but simultaneously heightens Washington's geopolitical anxiety: the tech embargo is not stopping China's progress. For investors, this is a signal to reassess valuations of companies whose capitalization rests solely on proprietary models. The market is moving toward a scenario where value shifts from model creation to applied implementation.
02

AST SpaceMobile BlueBird

▼
Direct-to-cell satellite technology threatens the traditional business model of terrestrial cell towers in remote regions. Partnerships with AT&T and Verizon show that telecom giants prefer to co-opt the threat rather than fight it. However, deploying such systems creates new challenges in data sovereignty and national security: the state loses control over the information flows of its citizens. In the long term, this strengthens the digital connectivity of the Global South, opening new markets for fintech and e-commerce.
03

Quantum Brilliance Quoll: A diamond quantum microprocessor

▼
Using defects in diamonds to create quantum microprocessors that operate at room temperature is a breakthrough in Edge Computing. Eliminating bulky cooling systems allows quantum tech to be integrated into autonomous systems, satellites, and military hardware. This reduces reliance on centralized cloud servers and power grids. Beneficiaries will be sectors with high demands for autonomy and data security. The technology also has dual-use potential, which will inevitably attract the attention of export control regulators.
04

Pindrop Pulse / Honor Deepfake Detector

▼
The explosive growth of deepfake fraud (causing millions in damages) is spawning a new market—the "reality verification industry." Implementing detectors at the device level (smartphones) and corporate communication systems is becoming a mandatory security standard. This creates a technological arms race between content generators and detectors. For business, the absence of such systems becomes a critical operational risk. Socially, this leads to a crisis of trust in all digital communications, increasing the value of physical presence and biometric identification.
05

Wildtype Salmon Saku

▼
FDA approval and technological progress in creating whole-cut cultivated meat (rather than ground meat) brings the technology closer to commercialization. However, lawsuits against sales bans (e.g., in Texas) expose the lobbying battle of the traditional agricultural sector against innovation. This is a classic example of conflict between the old and new economy: the meat lobby will use arguments of "cultural identity" and safety to stall competitors. For investors, this is a sector with high regulatory risk but immense potential in light of food security and climate issues.

NEWSWEEK SPECIAL COLLECTOR'S ISSUE

US Anniversary • Ecosystems • Attention Crisis • Market Cycles • Space
01

America Celebrates Its Semiquincentennial

▼
The grand celebration of the US 250th anniversary in 2026 is not just a tribute to history, but a tool for internal consolidation amidst political division. The federal center will use this jubilee to promote a narrative of unity and legitimize state institutions. Increased government spending on infrastructure and cultural projects related to the anniversary is expected. For business, this is an opportunity to participate in the "patriotism economy," securing contracts and tax breaks. However, there is a risk that the holiday will become an arena for ideological clashes between different political groups, each interpreting history to their advantage.
02

iPhone Introduced [2007 Moment Analysis]

▼
A retrospective on the iPhone launch in the context of 2025 highlights the end of the hardware innovation cycle and the transition to an ecosystem economy. The smartphone has evolved from a gadget into a digital identity verifier and the primary tool of consumption. The hidden logic of the market is now directed toward finding the "next platform" (neural interfaces, AR), as the smartphone market has reached saturation. The economy's dependence on a single product category (as with Apple) creates systemic risks for the stock market. Investors should view this historical moment as a reminder of the finiteness of any technological dominance.
03

First YouTube Video [2005 Moment Analysis]

▼
The evolution from the first amateur video to a dominant global platform illustrates the total victory of the algorithmic media model. Today, this sector faces a crisis of content overproduction and user fatigue. The hidden threat lies in the transition from the social graph to AI generation, which may devalue human creative labor. Regulatory pressure on platforms will intensify as their influence on population cognitive abilities and democratic processes is realized. The market is moving toward a model of paid verification and premium content consumption.
04

The California Gold Rush [1849 Moment Analysis]

▼
The historical parallel with the 1849 Gold Rush maps perfectly onto the current AI and crypto boom. Just as back then, the primary profits are extracted not by the "prospectors" (startups looking for AI applications), but by the "shovel sellers" (chip manufacturers, infrastructure, energy). This historical pattern warns investors of inevitable market consolidation and the bankruptcy of most race participants. The logic of the moment suggests seeking assets in adjacent sectors that ensure the functioning of the new economy, rather than in the speculative products themselves.
05

Space Moments Analysis

▼
The mention of past space achievements in 2025 serves as a reminder of the loss of state monopoly on space. The private sector (SpaceX, etc.) now dictates the pace of exploration, changing the risk structure: priority shifts from national prestige to commercial profitability. Geopolitically, this means the militarization of orbit and the struggle for resources on the Moon and asteroids. For investors, this signals the formation of a new legal and economic reality beyond Earth, where the rules of the game are not yet written, and the right of the strong (technologically advanced) will dominate.
