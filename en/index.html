DECEMBER 31, 2025 New Year's Edition
Ukraine denies Kremlin accusations, Trump's second term, Khaleda Zia's death, and the first American Pope. A global overview of the year's final day.

FINANCIAL TIMES

Ukraine • Private Equity • Yemen • Climate • Taiwan
01

Ukraine Scrambles to Disavow Alleged Attack on Putin

▼
The Kremlin is leveraging the alleged drone incident at Putin's residence as a tool of diplomatic pressure at a critical juncture in negotiations. Accusations against Kyiv, despite a lack of publicly presented evidence, provide Moscow with a convenient pretext to either freeze the negotiation track or justify an escalation of strikes on Ukrainian infrastructure. Donald Trump's reaction, expressing "fury" and accepting the Russian narrative, signals the fragility of support for Zelenskyy within the new US administration. For Kyiv, this creates a risk of being coerced into unfavorable concessions to maintain American aid. European allies find themselves sidelined as the security dialogue shifts toward bilateral Washington-Moscow contacts. Markets are pricing this rhetoric as a signal of heightened volatility, factoring in the risk of a peace process collapse. Russia is testing the White House's readiness to pressure its ally, setting a precedent for future manipulation.
02

Buyout Groups Selling Assets to Themselves at Record Pace

▼
The sharp rise in the use of "continuation vehicles" in private equity exposes structural liquidity issues within the industry. Unable to offload assets via IPOs or direct sales at desired valuations, funds are shifting companies from old portfolios to new ones, artificially locking in profits and generating fees. This creates an obvious conflict of interest where managers act as both buyers and sellers, potentially inflating valuations in the absence of market pricing. For institutional investors like pension funds, this carries the risk of concentrated losses and "zombie" portfolios. The surge in such deal volumes to $100 billion suggests an industry-wide attempt to defer recognizing true asset values. Regulators are beginning to scrutinize the opacity of these schemes, threatening stricter rules. In the long run, this could lead to a private equity market correction as valuation bubbles collide with reality.
03

Saudi Arabia Strikes Yemen Port to Halt Arms Smuggling

▼
Riyadh's direct airstrike on the port of Mukalla marks the transition of hidden tensions between Saudi Arabia and the UAE into open conflict. The struggle for control over Yemen's strategic ports reflects the competition between the two monarchies for economic dominance in the region and influence over maritime trade routes. The UAE, backing Southern separatists, aims to cement its presence in the Bab el-Mandeb Strait, clashing with Saudi interests that favor a unified Yemen under its patronage. This rift within the anti-Houthi coalition undermines US efforts to contain Iran and creates a security vacuum. For oil markets, this adds a risk premium, as threats to transit now stem from internal squabbles among Western allies. Washington faces a complex diplomatic mission to reconcile partners whose ambitions have outgrown their traditional alliance.
04

Climate Policy Falters Despite Clean Energy Boom

▼
The Trump administration's abandonment of key environmental regulations is triggering a global rollback in climate governance. The disconnect between the falling cost of green technology and state policy returning to hydrocarbon support creates uncertainty for long-term investments. US actions are forcing the EU and Canada to dilute their own standards to avoid losing industrial competitiveness, launching a "race to the bottom." This leads to market fragmentation, where different jurisdictions operate under incompatible rules. For multinational corporations, this means rising compliance risks and the need to duplicate supply chains. The refusal to coordinate climate efforts turns ecology from a sphere of cooperation into a tool of trade protectionism. Institutional erosion of the climate agenda increases future physical risks for infrastructure and the insurance sector.
05

China Ramps Up Military Drills Around Taiwan

▼
Large-scale PLA exercises practicing port blockades of Taiwan are becoming a tool of persistent pressure, eroding the status quo in the Strait. Trump's muted reaction, dismissing the maneuvers as unserious amidst claims of friendship with Xi Jinping, may be perceived by Beijing as a green light for greater freedom of action. This heightens the risk of miscalculation, as China may intensify "grey zone" tactics without fear of an immediate US response. For Japan and other regional allies, Washington's stance incentivizes accelerated militarization and the search for new security frameworks. The tech sector receives another reminder of semiconductor supply chain vulnerability, accelerating manufacturing diversification. Beijing is effectively normalizing its military presence around the island, turning drills into a lever for the economic strangulation of Taipei.

THE INDIAN EXPRESS

Bangladesh • Russian Oil • Modi • Uttar Pradesh • Mumbai
01

Death of Khaleda Zia Sparks Political Uncertainty in Bangladesh

▼
The passing of the Bangladesh Nationalist Party (BNP) leader comes at a moment of critical power transition in Dhaka. The death of the opposition matriarch and the return of her son, Tarique Rahman, consolidates party ranks but opens a period of struggle for legacy and influence. For India, this event poses strategic challenges: historically complex relations with the BNP and the absence of Sheikh Hasina require New Delhi to forge new communication channels. There is a risk of rising Islamist sentiment if the power vacuum is not filled by stable institutions. The presence of India's External Affairs Minister at the funeral is a pragmatic attempt to maintain influence and prevent the neighbor from drifting into China or Pakistan's orbit. Domestic instability in Bangladesh could negatively impact regional trade projects and border security.
02

Russian Oil Supplies: New Players and the Shadow Fleet

▼
The emergence of obscure intermediaries from the UAE in Russian oil supply chains evidences Moscow's adaptation to US sanctions against Rosneft and Lukoil. The displacement of major traders and supply fragmentation through shell companies makes the market opaque and complicates price cap monitoring. For India, this means continued access to discounted crude, but at the cost of exponentially higher secondary sanction risks for its financial sector. The shadow fleet is becoming a key logistics element, raising environmental and insurance risks. Russia demonstrates an ability to bypass restrictions by building a parallel financial infrastructure. However, reliance on unvetted counterparties could lead to supply disruptions and price volatility in India's domestic market.
03

PM Modi Expresses Concern Over Attack on Putin's Residence

▼
Narendra Modi's emphasized concern regarding reports of the attack on Putin's residence demonstrates India's desire to maintain a trusted dialogue with Moscow. New Delhi effectively legitimizes the Russian version of events to protect its strategic interests in energy and defense. This move allows India to position itself as an independent player capable of speaking to all sides of the conflict, but risks irritating Kyiv and the West. The call for diplomacy serves the goal of preventing uncontrolled escalation that could hit the global economy. India signals it is not ready to join the isolation of Russia, despite pressure. Modi attempts to convert neutrality into geopolitical capital, offering India as a mediator for the Global South.
04

Voter List Delays in Uttar Pradesh Raise Eyebrows

▼
Repeated postponements of voter list publication and the projected removal of nearly 19% of the electorate in a key state raise suspicions of pre-election manipulation. Large-scale purging of lists may be aimed at excluding disloyal demographic groups, casting doubt on the integrity of the electoral process. Using administrative resources to shape a "desirable" electoral landscape undermines trust in democratic institutions. High deletion rates in urban constituencies point to a targeted campaign against the opposition. This creates risks of social tension and protests following the results. For investors, this signals that political stability is being secured not by consensus, but by control, which may result in long-term turbulence.
05

Mumbai Municipal Polls: Funds Flow to Ruling Alliance

▼
An investigation into the distribution of municipal funds in Mumbai has revealed a disparity: 99% of development funds are directed to wards controlled by the ruling alliance. This turns fiscal policy into a tool of political coercion, placing opposition districts under conditions of artificial resource scarcity. Financial strangulation of opponents ahead of elections undermines competition and principles of local self-governance. The concentration of resources allows the ruling party to cement its power, using urban infrastructure as an electoral bonus. For business, this means contract access will be strictly conditional on political loyalty. The situation in the financial capital reflects a broader trend toward power centralization and the use of economic levers to suppress political competition.

TIME (SPECIAL EDITION)

Trump 2.0 • Middle East • AI • Healthcare • Pope Leo XIV
01

Trump's Return and the Erosion of Norms

▼
Trump's second term marks a systemic rejection of established norms in American democracy and foreign policy. The willingness to use state apparatus against domestic opponents and the revision of international alliances create a precedent for the authoritarian transformation of US institutions. For the global system, this means the end of predictable American leadership and a shift to transactional diplomacy. Markets face risks of sudden protectionist measures and trade wars, unchecked by traditional advisors. Social polarization in the US is reaching levels that threaten civil peace. Trump is testing the limits of presidential power, which could lead to a long-term weakening of checks and balances.
02

Middle East Peace Deal: A Fragile Hope

▼
The achieved ceasefire and hostage release represent a tactical pause, not a strategic resolution to the conflict. The destruction of Gaza and the humanitarian catastrophe create fertile ground for the radicalization of future generations, despite the temporary lull. The deal, pushed through by the Trump administration under threat of force, may prove unsustainable without addressing the fundamental Palestinian question. Israel gains a respite but remains in a complex situation of international isolation and dependence on US guarantees. Regional players are forced to adapt to a reality where Washington dictates terms while ignoring multilateral mechanisms. The risk of resumed hostilities remains high as the root causes of the conflict persist.
03

The Future of AI: Golden Age or Existential Risk?

▼
The lack of a unified approach to AI regulation creates risks of uncontrolled technological development capable of destabilizing markets and society. Tech giants, given carte blanche by the Trump administration, are concentrating power comparable to state actors without due accountability. The gap between investor optimism and scientists' warnings about existential threats amplifies uncertainty. The geopolitical AI arms race makes international security cooperation nearly impossible. Automation threatens social upheavals for which state institutions are unprepared. Humanity finds itself hostage to corporate ambitions, where deployment speed takes precedence over safety.
04

Healthcare Crisis and RFK Jr.'s Reforms

▼
The appointment of a vaccine skeptic to lead US health agencies and mass layoffs at scientific bodies (NIH, CDC) undermine the national biodefense system. The politicization of science and funding cuts for research create long-term risks for public health and pandemic preparedness. This could lead to a brain drain and reduced innovation potential in the US pharmaceutical industry. State-level propagation of misinformation threatens to lower herd immunity and bring back forgotten diseases. Economic consequences include lower labor productivity and rising treatment costs. The private sector faces an unpredictable regulatory environment where decisions are driven by ideology rather than evidence-based medicine.
05

New Pope Leo XIV and Vatican Geopolitics

▼
The election of the first American Pope changes the balance of power in a Catholic Church traditionally oriented toward Europe or the Global South. Leo XIV faces the complex task of proving his independence from Washington's politics while maintaining the role of a global moral arbiter. His attempts at mediation in conflicts indicate the Vatican's desire to fill the diplomatic vacuum amidst a crisis of international institutions. However, the Pontiff's nationality may complicate relations with China and nations critical of the US. The Vatican is trying to adapt to a multipolar world but risks being perceived as an instrument of Western "soft power." Internal church reforms may stall as he balances between liberal and conservative wings.
