MONEYWEEK

Silver, Europe, Unilever, Meta, China.
1

Silver surges to a new record peak

▶
Silver's rally past $60 per ounce is driven by a dual pressure: industrial demand from the EV and solar sectors, and the metal's role as a hedge against currency devaluation. For investors, this signals a structural market deficit exacerbated by stagnant mining output and depleting stockpiles. Mining companies and holders of physical assets benefit, while high-tech manufacturers face rising input costs. Geopolitically, this strengthens the hand of raw material exporting nations, creating new leverage in trade wars. While correction risks exist, fundamentals point to a long-term commodity "supercycle."
2

Europe's economic sunset (Hubris, meet nemesis: A long decline)

▶
Statistics showing the EU's share of the global economy shrinking from 25% to 14% over a quarter-century underscore a systemic crisis in the European integration model. A single currency without a unified fiscal policy has created structural imbalances, benefiting the export-oriented North (Germany) while stifling the South. The lack of a unified services market and capital fragmentation hamper the emergence of European tech giants capable of competing with the US and China. For investors, this is a signal to be cautious about European assets, barring niche sectors. Geopolitically, a weak Europe becomes an object, rather than a subject, in the Washington-Beijing standoff.
3

Unilever slims down: Ice cream spinoff

▶
Unilever's decision to spin off its ice cream division (including Ben & Jerry’s) into a standalone entity, Magnum Ice Cream Company, is driven by a desire to shed low-margin assets with complex logistics. This allows the parent company to improve profitability metrics and focus on more stable personal care and household segments. For the new company, risks include volatile raw material prices (cocoa) and the loss of the giant's "umbrella" protection. Politically, Unilever also sheds reputational risks associated with Ben & Jerry’s activism. Markets view this as classic portfolio optimization under shareholder pressure.
4

Meta finally gives up on the metaverse

▶
Budget cuts to the Reality Labs division and a pivot to AI and wearables (smart glasses) signal Mark Zuckerberg's admission of a $70 billion strategic error. Investors receive a signal of the company's return to pragmatism and profit generation, positively impacting stock performance. Beneficiaries include AI component manufacturers and app developers, while the VR content market faces consolidation and capital flight. This move also reflects a global trend: tech giants are reallocating resources from hypothetical future platforms to technologies delivering immediate impact (AI).
5

China’s trade surplus soars past $1 trillion

▶
China's record trade surplus, despite tariff wars with the US, indicates a successful pivot of exports to the Global South and Europe. Beijing is compensating for falling Western demand through aggressive expansion into developing markets, utilizing dumping and state subsidies. This creates risks of rising protectionism not just in the US, but also in the EU and Asian nations fearing for their own industries. For global markets, this means intensifying imbalances and currency wars. China's dominance in "green tech" supply chains turns trade into a tool of geopolitical leverage.

NEWSWEEK

Vance & Trump, Israel, Surrogacy, Russia-India, ESG.
1

JD Vance prepares for the post-Trump era

▶
Vice President Vance is consciously avoiding narrow portfolios (like immigration for Harris), positioning himself as the universal ideologue of the MAGA movement. This is a long-term survival strategy: he aims to inherit Trump's base in 2028, minimizing the risks of current administration failures. Beneficiaries include young technocrats and the "New Right," who see him as a conduit for their interests for decades. The risk lies in his total tethering to Trump's ratings: any crisis of presidential legitimacy will boomerang on Vance. For markets, this signals potential continuity of protectionist and isolationist policies in the long run.
2

Israel seeks peace with Saudi Arabia via Trump

▶
Israeli President Herzog is leveraging Trump's return to revive the "Abraham Accords," seeking normalization with Riyadh while bypassing the Palestinian issue. This benefits Israel's defense and tech sectors gaining access to Gulf capital, and the US administration aiming to build an anti-Iran bloc. However, Saudi Arabia demands security guarantees and nuclear technology, creating proliferation risks in the region. The marginalization of the Palestinian side threatens new violence that could derail any deal. Geopolitically, the deal cements the Middle East's division into pro-American and pro-Iranian camps.
3

Is the Surrogacy Boom About to Burst?

▶
A UN initiative to ban commercial surrogacy is uniting conservatives and radical feminists, creating a powerful lobbying front against a $22 billion industry. This threatens agencies and clinics in the US and developing nations but benefits countries with strict regulations aiming to curb "reproductive tourism." For the market, this signals potential regulatory risks and rising service costs. The social aspect involves a re-evaluation of the ethics of commodifying the human body, which may drive demand into grey zones.
4

Russia and India strengthen economic ties (Power Duo)

▶
Putin's visit to India and a deal to expand trade to $100 billion by 2030 demonstrate the failure of Western attempts to isolate Moscow. New Delhi is exploiting the situation to secure cheap energy and technology, balancing between West and East. This benefits Russian raw material exporters and the Indian industrial sector. For the US, it is a strategic defeat showing the limits of sanctions pressure. Geopolitically, a stable Eurasian axis is forming, reducing the efficacy of the dollar-based financial system.
5

Corporate Social Responsibility as an asset (America's Most Responsible Companies 2026)

▶
The ranking of socially responsible companies reflects a shift in investment paradigms: ESG factors are becoming risk management tools, not just marketing. Highly rated companies gain access to cheaper capital and consumer loyalty, critical during inflation. However, "greenwashing" risks remain where declarations diverge from reality. Investors use this data to screen assets, aiming to avoid reputational scandals. Long-term, this increases pressure on companies ignoring environmental and social standards.

THE EUROPEAN BUSINESS REVIEW

AI in Medicine, AI Ethics, Business Education, HR Tech, Logistics.
1

Dentistry's AI Revolution: Lessons for Other Industries

▶
Successful AI integration in the conservative dentistry sector demonstrates the importance of embedding technology into existing workflows rather than disrupting them. Dental Service Organizations (DSOs) and insurers benefit from reduced costs and higher diagnostic accuracy. For investors, this is a case study on how AI monetizes via higher average tickets and staff efficiency. Risks lie in over-reliance on algorithms and potential over-diagnosis for profit. This signals to other sectors (construction, law) that AI success depends on seamless implementation, not hype.
2

Nine Logics of AI Deployments and the Artificial Integrity Imperative

▶
Analyzing AI deployment through the lens of "integrity" reveals hidden risks in strategies aimed solely at headcount reduction. Companies choosing "Rationalize AI" gain short-term profit but lose institutional resilience and innovation potential. "Empower AI" creates new markets but requires retraining investment. For C-suites, this is a warning: ignoring the human factor in AI adoption leads to fragile business models. Investors should evaluate not just AI savings, but the impact on long-term viability.
3

Following the Twin Paths to Knowledge in Business Education

▶
The Antai College model, combining academic research with industry research, reflects the Chinese economy's demand for practical knowledge application. This benefits corporations gaining access to talent and analytics, and the university enhancing its influence. For Western business schools, this is a challenge: the Chinese education model is becoming more integrated into the real sector. Strategically, this strengthens China's human capital sovereignty amidst technological blockades. The risk lies in the potential loss of academic independence to corporate interests.
4

Algorithmic Attachment: How AI-Based Employee Monitoring Shapes Workplace Trust

▶
Implementing AI employee monitoring through the lens of attachment theory exposes psychological risks of automated management. Employees with anxious attachment styles perceive monitoring as distrust, leading to burnout and reduced efficiency. This creates hidden costs: rising turnover and falling loyalty. For HR directors, this signals a need to humanize algorithms. Investors should account for risks of toxic corporate culture devaluing human capital.
5

Market, Logistics, and Digital Technology in Yiwu

▶
Yiwu's resilience as a global small commodities hub is secured by deep integration of digital platforms and logistics. This allows China to maintain dominance in consumer goods supply chains despite geopolitical barriers. Beneficiaries are Chinese SMEs and logistics giants. For global competitors, this demonstrates how a digital ecosystem can mitigate tariff restrictions. Risks are tied to global demand dependence, but the Yiwu model's flexibility allows rapid pivoting to new markets.

THE GUARDIAN WEEKLY

Social Media Ban, Syria, Ukraine, Trump & EU, Gaza.
1

Blocked! Why Australia banned kids from social media

▶
Australia's ban on social media for under-16s sets a global precedent threatening Big Tech business models (Meta, TikTok). This benefits traditional media (News Corp), which lobbied for the law, and politicians scoring points on child protection. For tech firms, this is a serious regulatory risk: losing the "Generation Alpha" audience undermines the long-term user base. Risks include teens moving to unregulated platforms or using VPNs, negating the ban's effect. Geopolitically, this marks the start of global internet fragmentation by age censorship.
2

Syria: One year after Assad

▶
A year after the Assad regime's fall, Syria remains a zone of instability where new authorities try to balance Western support and internal contradictions. Legitimizing former rebel leader al-Sharaa via US contacts benefits Washington as a tool to contain Iran but solves no internal justice issues. This creates risks of a new civil war based on sectarian revenge. Regional investors should account for continuing institutional fragility. Geopolitically, Syria is becoming a testing ground for "managed democratization" of former jihadists.
3

Fight on, say Kyiv's weary frontline troops

▶
Frontline reports indicate critical manpower depletion in the Ukrainian Armed Forces amidst Western diplomatic pressure. Soldiers are willing to fight, but lack of rotation and weaponry undermines combat effectiveness. This benefits the Kremlin, betting on prolonged conflict and demoralization. For Kyiv, the risk is being forced into an unfavorable peace due to resource exhaustion. Markets perceive this as a signal for a potential conflict freeze, which could temporarily lower the geopolitical risk premium on energy.
4

Trump’s global rampage is starting to defy description

▶
Rhetoric from Trump and his advisors about "decaying Europe" and support for right-wing populists signals a fundamental review of the transatlantic partnership. The US is shifting to bilateral deals, ignoring EU and NATO institutions. This benefits Russia, gaining an opportunity to split Western unity. For European business, this carries risks of losing the US market and the defense umbrella. Investors should prepare for rising European defense spending and increased economic fragmentation.
5

"Yellow line" in Gaza is new border

▶
The de facto partition of the Gaza Strip into control zones ("Yellow Line") cements Israeli military presence indefinitely. This contradicts official ceasefire agreements but benefits the Israeli right wing seeking territorial annexation. For Palestinians, it means turning the enclave into a system of isolated camps with no reconstruction prospects. The risk lies in population radicalization and impossible long-term stabilization. Geopolitically, this buries the "two-state" solution, creating a permanent flashpoint.

THE WEEK US

Venezuela, Inflation, Supreme Court, Vaccines, Robots.
1

Democrats demand release of full boat-strike video

▶
Demands to release video of the killing of survivors after a strike on a Venezuelan boat expose the Trump administration to war crimes risks. This is domestic political warfare: Democrats aim to delegitimize the White House's aggressive foreign policy. The Maduro regime may benefit, using the incident to mobilize anti-American sentiment. For the Pentagon, this risks restrictions on covert ops. Markets are watching for escalation, as direct US intervention in Venezuela would impact oil prices.
2

Trump dismisses affordability as a 'hoax'

▶
Trump's claims that the cost-of-living crisis is exaggerated, amidst real inflation, show a disconnect between political rhetoric and economic reality. This attempt to shift blame to predecessors and migrants risks alienating middle-class voters. Democrats benefit, gaining ammunition for the 2026 campaign. For markets, this signals the administration may ignore structural economic issues for political slogans. The risk lies in ignoring real purchasing power decline.
3

Supreme Court set to expand presidential power

▶
The Supreme Court's readiness to overturn a 1935 precedent and allow the President to fire independent agency heads (except the Fed) marks a shift to the "unitary executive" model. This benefits Trump, seeking political control over regulators (FTC, SEC). For business, this carries risks of politically motivated regulation and instability. Institutionally, it undermines checks and balances. Markets may react nervously to the loss of independence for key economic regulators.
4

Will Kennedy dismantle U.S. immunization policy?

▶
A decision by a CDC committee staffed with RFK Jr. supporters to limit Hepatitis B vaccination for newborns marks a victory for the anti-vax lobby over evidence-based medicine. This creates long-term public health risks and costs for the insurance system covering preventable diseases. Alternative medicine movements and Kennedy's political base benefit. For pharma, this signals the start of a regulatory war against vaccines. Social risk: the return of forgotten epidemics.
5

The robot revolution

▶
Forecasts of 1.3 billion robots by 2035 and Amazon's plans to replace 600,000 jobs point to inevitable mass automation. Tech giants and robotics makers (Tesla, 1X) benefit by reducing labor dependence. For the labor market, this risks structural unemployment and social tension. Economically, it promises productivity growth but raises distribution questions. Geopolitically, leadership in robotics (where China competes actively) becomes a national security factor.
