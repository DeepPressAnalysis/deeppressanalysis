FINANCIAL TIMES

Japan Defense • Big Tech Debt • EU Estrangement
The Liberal Democratic Party of Japan under the leadership of Sanae Takaichi has secured a constitutional supermajority, winning more than two-thirds of the seats. This unties the Prime Minister's hands to revise the "peace" Article 9 of the 1947 Constitution, signaling a definitive rejection of post-war pacifism and a move toward full remilitarization of Japan. The internal mandate allows Takaichi to ignore opposition criticism and accelerate the buildup of defense spending, which benefits the national military-industrial complex and strengthens Tokyo's position in containing China. For markets, this is a signal of the beginning of an era of aggressive fiscal stimulus and "expansive" state spending, which may support the Japanese stock market but will increase the debt load. Geopolitically, this consolidates the pro-American bloc in Asia, reducing US dependence on its own resources in the region.
Tech giants like Amazon, Meta, and Alphabet have encountered a situation where capital expenditures on AI infrastructure exceed operating cash flow, forcing them to enter debt markets. The expected bond issuance of over $500 billion signals a transition from an "asset-light" model to capital-intensive production, which carries risks for dividend policy and share buybacks. The corporate debt market may face an oversupply, leading to widening credit spreads. For investors, this is a test of faith in AI payback: if new capacities do not yield quick monetization, the technology sector faces a severe valuation repricing.
Deep disappointment in the transatlantic partnership is growing in Germany against the backdrop of the Trump administration's policy and cultural shifts in the States themselves. The risk of withdrawal of American troops or reduction of presence at the Ramstein base threatens not only the security of Europe but also local economies that have serviced US military infrastructure for decades. Politically, this pushes Berlin to search for European strategic autonomy; however, real dependence on the American "umbrella" remains critical. For the EU, this is an existential challenge: the need for a sharp increase in its own defense budgets amidst economic stagnation and the absence of guarantees from Washington.
Brazil is becoming a battlefield between the US, the EU, and China for access to rare earth elements necessary for the "green transition" and the military-industrial complex. Washington and Brussels are trying to displace China, which dominates supply chains, offering investments in exchange for raw material security. For Brazil, this is an opportunity to dictate terms and attract capital, playing on the contradictions of superpowers. However, there is a risk of increased pressure from the US demanding a limitation of Chinese presence, which may place Brasilia before a difficult geopolitical choice and the threat of trade restrictions.
Economists are skeptical of the thesis of Trump's candidate for Fed head Kevin Warsh that the productivity boom from AI will allow for painless rate cuts. The consensus of experts points out that the inflationary effect from AI in the short term will be insignificant, and Warsh's argumentation is perceived as a politically motivated attempt to justify the soft monetary policy desired by the White House. This creates a risk of undermining trust in the independence of the Fed and may lead to volatility in debt markets if investors decide that the regulator is acting to please the political cycle, not economic realities.

THE NEW YORK TIMES

US Politics • Cuba Crisis • Clinton/Epstein
The American team in Italy faces an unprecedented hostile reception from the stands, caused by the foreign policy of the Trump administration and its rhetoric regarding Europe. Athletes become hostages of the country's geopolitical image: any patriotic gesture is perceived as support for the White House's course, while distancing causes the President's anger in social networks. This creates a toxic atmosphere around the team and undermines US "soft power." For the IOC and sponsors, this is a signal that sport has finally ceased to be a neutral zone, which carries reputational risks for brands associated with US national symbolism.
The Trump administration is implementing a strategy of "maximum pressure" on Havana, cutting off oil supplies and tourist flows with the aim of provoking the regime's collapse by the end of 2026. The calculation is made that economic collapse will lead to a social explosion that will sweep away the communist government that has stood for over 60 years. However, this strategy carries risks of uncontrolled chaos at US borders and a new migration wave that Florida may not withstand. For the region, this means increased instability and Cuba's loss of agency, and for investors — a ghostly, but potentially high-yield prospect of opening a closed market in the event of a change of power.
New documents confirm the direct participation of Ghislaine Maxwell in the launch of the Clinton Global Initiative, which strikes a blow to the reputation of the Democratic establishment. Financial ties and transfers approved by Epstein point to a deeper integration of toxic figures into elite structures than previously admitted. This weakens the Democrats' positions on the eve of electoral cycles, giving trump cards to Republicans for attacks on the "corrupt elite." For the charity sector, this is a risk of tightening checks on funding sources and donor toxicity, which may complicate fundraising for global projects.
The President's deletion of a racist post about the Obamas and subsequent attempts to justify himself indicate the limits of his political invulnerability. Even the loyal Republican Party demonstrates signs of fatigue from the need to constantly defend the leader's personal attacks, which creates cracks in monolithic support. This is a signal for opponents and intra-party competitors that pressure on pain points (the racial question, ethics) can bring tactical victories. For markets and institutions, this is evidence that, despite aggressive rhetoric, the administration is sensitive to coordinated public and elite indignation.
The conflict between the Hennepin County Sheriff and the federal immigration service (ICE) illustrates the crisis of federalism and Washington's use of power resources to coerce regions. The local sheriff's refusal to cooperate with the feds puts her career and freedom at risk under the pressure of DOJ lawsuits, but consolidates the protest electorate. For states, this is a precedent of struggle for the autonomy of police functions. If the federal center pushes its line through, this will create a mechanism for the direct subordination of local law enforcers to the political will of the White House, which fundamentally changes the balance of forces in the American law enforcement system.

THE ECONOMIST

Press Freedom • Dollar Dangers • Nuclear Reality
Analysis shows a direct correlation between restricted free speech and rising embezzlement: politicians silence critics to steal with impunity, creating a vicious cycle. Democracies adopting autocratic methods (financial strangulation of independent media, lawsuits) risk sliding into institutional degradation. For business, this is an alarming signal: lack of transparency and public oversight inevitably leads to higher corruption costs and regulatory unpredictability. Long-term, such regimes become economically inefficient despite the apparent stability of a "strong hand."
The dollar is losing ground (down 10% in a year) against the backdrop of Trump's policy combining tariff wars, pressure on the Fed, and ignoring the budget deficit. Foreign investors are starting to perceive American assets as risky, evidenced by rising gold prices and reserve diversification. The decline of the dollar's role as a safe haven threatens the US privilege of financing debt externally. If the trend holds, America faces rising borrowing costs and imported inflation, while the global financial system risks fragmentation and a search for alternative settlement units.
The world is shifting from a non-proliferation regime to a logic where nuclear weapons are considered the only guarantee of sovereignty. With US security guarantees in doubt (especially in Asia and Europe), allies like South Korea and Poland may start their own nuclear programs. This raises accidental conflict risks and destroys Cold War strategic stability. For the defense industry, it means more missile defense and strategic weapon orders, but for the global economy, a permanent geopolitical risk premium.
The Peter Mandelson-Epstein scandal in Britain hits Keir Starmer's Labour government, undermining its moral authority. Mandelson's resignation from the Lords doesn't answer questions about elite ties and insider info exchange during crises. This fuels voter cynicism and aids populists (Reform UK) positioning themselves against a rotten system. For the City of London, it risks stricter compliance and a review of informal influence channels traditionally linking politics and big business.
Merging SpaceX and xAI to create space-based data centers demonstrates Elon Musk's ambition for an autonomous tech ecosystem independent of ground grids and jurisdictions. This gives Musk global leverage comparable to a state, given Starlink and AI roles in military and civil communications. Concentrating critical infrastructure in one private individual's hands creates national security risks for the US and others potentially dependent on a billionaire's whims.

THE GUARDIAN UK

Labour Crisis • Refugee Policy • Thailand Election
Morgan McSweeney's resignation strips PM Starmer of his chief strategist and the architect of his rise to power. Labour faces a management crisis amid the Mandelson scandal, leaving the PM vulnerable to party rebellion. Weakened central authority may paralyze decision-making and embolden the left wing demanding radical social measures. For business, this spells uncertainty regarding government direction as the Downing Street power balance is disrupted.
Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood's tough stance limiting refugee family reunions splits Labour and draws criticism from party veterans like Lord Dubs. The government's attempt to co-opt the right-wing populist agenda (Reform UK) erodes liberal values and alienates the base. This risks internal instability and rights protests. Strategically, it shows migration remains a toxic asset in British politics, forcing even a center-left government into right-wing methods.
The swift extradition of a suspect from the UAE to Russia demonstrates Moscow's enduring Middle East influence and the effectiveness of its special services in pursuing enemies abroad. The attempt on GRU General Alekseev highlights Russian elite vulnerability even in the rear, but also the Kremlin's readiness to use diplomatic levers for suspect return. Theories of a "Ukrainian trace" or "internal feuds" are used to tighten counter-intelligence. For regional players, it signals Dubai is no safe haven for Kremlin adversaries.
Revealed undercounting of deaths from nitazenes (stronger than heroin) exposes a hidden UK public health crisis. This indicates monitoring and rehab systems are unprepared for new synthetic drugs flooding the market. Social consequences will hit the poorest hardest, straining the NHS and social services. Ignoring the problem risks social explosion and crime growth linked to drug trafficking.
The establishment victory in Thailand cements military-royalist elite power and extinguishes youth hopes for democratization. Regime stability is backed by army and big business but at the cost of rising social tension and brain drain. For investors, it means status quo and predictable rules short-term, but preserves structural economic problems and political unrest risks long-term if election legitimacy is questioned.

THE WALL STREET JOURNAL

Welfare Scandal • Crypto Conflicts • AI Bots
Investigation reveals systemic failure in oversight of the TANF program ($30 billion), with states massively diverting funds to non-poverty goals (from stadiums to anti-abortion centers). This benefits local elites of both parties plugging budget holes and funding loyal NGOs. Exposing abuse scale risks federal intervention and block grant review, hitting state fiscal autonomy. Socially, it means the real safety net for the poor is broken, exacerbating inequality.
The participation of children of Trump's inner circle in launching the President-backed World Liberty Financial crypto project creates a clear conflict of interest. This is pure monetization of political influence, where investors buy access to power, not tech. The risk is that project collapse (given crypto volatility) hits not just wallets but administration political capital. It also signals regulators that certain crypto segments are untouchable, distorting competition.
The emergence of an AI-agent social network (Moltbook), where bots develop their own "religions" and languages, raises questions about unpredictable emergent algorithm behavior. While some activity may be simulation, autonomous machine interaction creates risks of losing control over information flows. For cybersecurity, it's a nightmare scenario: botnets self-organizing for attacks without human orders. Commercially, it opens a "machine economy" niche where transactions occur without people.
A whistleblower complaint against DNI Tulsi Gabbard for suppressing intercepts on Iran and Trump's circle indicates continuing intel community warfare. Politicization undermines trust in objective reporting to the President, risking decisions based on distorted reality. For allies, it signals unreliable US info sharing channels. Internal sabotage or leaks become political struggle tools, paralyzing agencies.
Volatility in software stocks and a quick market bounce mask growing fears over giant AI capex returns. "Price insensitivity" in sell-offs indicates nervousness among major players ready to dump assets at first sign of trouble. The risk is that if Big Tech earnings don't show explosive AI profit growth, correction could become a full bear trend dragging down the market. Investors seek "old economy" safety, fearing the tech bubble is near its limit.

THE WASHINGTON POST

Nihilism Terror • Retirement Crisis • Papal Policy
The emergence of a category of nihilist terrorists who make no demands but seek civilization collapse baffles intelligence services. Traditional profiling methods (seeking political motives) fail. This is a symptom of deep social alienation and psychic crisis fueled by online "accelerationism" culture. For society, it is a permanent threat of unpredictable violence unsolvable by political concessions. Authorities must rethink internet monitoring and mental health strategies, risking increased digital control.
CMS Administrator Mehmet Oz's initiative to raise retirement age is pitched as economic prudence but ignores class divides. For white-collar workers, it extends active life; for laborers, it is work until death. Economic logic (deficit reduction, GDP growth) clashes with social inequality in life expectancy. Politically, it's a "ticking bomb" for Republicans affecting their Rust Belt base, and a gift to Democrats attacking administration "elitism."
The White House bets on a "trifecta" (tax cuts, rate cuts, deregulation) to create an artificial boom before midterms. The goal is to offset inflation/culture war negativity with income growth. However, overheating the economy risks an inflationary spiral post-election. The Fed faces immense pressure to sync policy with the electoral cycle. If the bet pays off, Republicans keep Congress, but at the cost of future macroeconomic imbalances.
Pope Leo XIV's decision not to visit the US (as the first American Pope) is a demonstrative gesture of distancing from the wealthy American church and the Trump administration. It signals a Global South priority and reluctance to associate with US polarization. For conservative Catholics, it's a disappointment; for liberals, confirmation of papal independence. Strategically, the Vatican avoids the trap of becoming "Chaplain of the West," maintaining global influence through neutrality.
Loss and destruction of New Deal era murals in Post Offices symbolizes erosion of public good and historical memory. The Postal Service, acting corporately, sheds "non-core assets," erasing heritage created for the people. Debates on painting over "offensive" themes (e.g., Black labor) show how culture wars sterilize history. This benefits developers and privatizers but strips society of visual identity anchors and state care symbols.
