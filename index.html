IN FOCUS TODAY: US 'Predatory Hegemony' doctrine, Autonomous AI Agents displacing SaaS, UAE's logistics empire, India's strategic balancing, the cognitive obsolescence of the middle class, and the new 'Centaur' labor paradigm.

FOREIGN AFFAIRS

US Hegemony • Geopolitics • EU Defense
Washington's strategy in Trump's second term has transformed from "isolationism" to "predatory hegemony." The White House no longer views alliances as assets requiring investment, but as a resource base for direct profit extraction. The logic is simple: using allies' (Europe, Asia) dependence on US security guarantees to impose exploitative economic conditions. This creates risks for multinational corporations accustomed to stable rules: now any asset can become a bargaining chip in bilateral trade. For markets, this signals long-term currency volatility for US satellite nations and capital flight to "gray zones." Geopolitically, this pushes even loyal regimes to seek alternative power centers, accelerating global security fragmentation.
The article exposes the mechanism of merging US foreign policy with private business interests of the ruling elite. It's not just corruption, but the systemic use of diplomacy for personal enrichment via "package deals" (arms-for-investments). Lifting tech export restrictions or peace deals are now directly linked to sovereign wealth fund investments (Saudi Arabia, UAE) in projects affiliated with the president's family. This creates colossal compliance risks for global business: participating in seemingly legal deals can toxicify assets upon administration change. Institutionally, this destroys financial monitoring and sanctions control mechanisms. For investors, this is a signal: due diligence must now include analysis of informal ties to the White House.
Analysis confirms the final collapse of supranational governance as an effective tool for global problem-solving. The focus shifts to sovereign nation-states as the only capable actors. This means dismantling remaining global institutions (UN, WTO, WHO) and shifting to direct "strong-weak" bilateral agreements. For transnational business, this entails rising localization costs and the need to build separate strategies for each jurisdiction. The logic of a "single market" yields to the logic of a "secured perimeter." Strategically, countries with large domestic markets and resource self-sufficiency win, while export-oriented economies of small nations face maximum risk.
The scenario of a "managed retreat" by the US to the "first island chain" (Japan, Taiwan, Philippines) is becoming the baseline. Factually, this means Washington abandoning commitments to mainland allies and recognizing China's sphere of influence in Southeast Asia. This radically alters supply chain risk maps: the Strait of Malacca and South China Sea de facto pass under Beijing's control. Japan and South Korea will be forced to either accelerate their own nuclear programs or seek forms of vassalage with China. For investors, this signals an asset withdrawal from "buffer zones" (Vietnam, Thailand) and concentration in defended, militarized enclaves.
Europe finds itself in a strategic vacuum: the US is folding its "security umbrella" while Russia ramps up pressure on NATO's eastern flank. The hidden logic points to the inevitability of a direct clash for which the EU is institutionally and militarily unprepared. Germany, as the economic engine, becomes the most vulnerable link due to dependence on external markets and lack of strategic autonomy. This creates existential risks for the euro and European debt securities. Beneficiaries are the US military-industrial complex receiving panic orders, and US energy companies replacing Russian supplies. For global capital, Europe moves from "safe haven" to "high-risk asset."

AI BUSINESS MAGAZINE

Autonomous Agents • M&A • Sovereign AI
The publication notes a shift from generative AI (chatbots) to agentic AI (executors). Agents now autonomously make decisions, manage budgets, and close deals within ecosystems. This leads to mass reductions in middle management, whose function was control and coordination. For corporations, this means a sharp OPEX reduction but creates new risks of losing control: "black box" algorithms begin determining operational strategy. Investment focus shifts from LLM developers to creators of infrastructure for autonomous agents. The main risk is legal liability for AI actions, which remains in a "gray zone."
M&A processes are radically accelerating: what took auditors months, AI does in hours. This changes capital market dynamics, allowing aggressive real-time takeovers. However, the hidden risk lies in algorithmic bias and data manipulation: if both sides use similar valuation models, the market becomes vulnerable to synchronous errors. Traditional consulting giants lose market share to fintech platforms with built-in AI audit. For CEOs, this is a tool for instant expansion, but for shareholders, it's a risk of buying a "pig in a poke" if AI misses a non-standard threat.
The software market is transforming: instead of selling tools (SaaS), companies are starting to sell results (Service-as-a-Result). Clients pay not for CRM access, but for the number of closed deals executed by an AI agent. This breaks the traditional subscription business model and requires revaluation of all tech companies. Winners are providers with access to unique proprietary data for training agents. This reinforces monopolization: only giants can afford to create effective industry agents. For small business, this lowers entry barriers (no need to hire staff) but creates total dependence on platform providers.
States and large corporations are building isolated "AI factories" (full-cycle data centers) to ensure data sovereignty. The global internet is fracturing into "digital fiefdoms" where access to computing power becomes the main strategic resource. This creates a boom in demand for energy and hardware but fragments the knowledge market. For investors, companies providing autonomous infrastructure (energy, chips, cooling) are of interest. Geopolitically, this leads to a "computing arms race," where lagging in data center capacity equates to losing national sovereignty.
AI is completely taking over the recruiting process: from sourcing to initial interviews. This solves the talent shortage for small business but creates a systemic risk of algorithmic discrimination that is impossible to track. The labor market becomes transparent to algorithms but opaque to humans: applicants are forced to "optimize" themselves for machines. This changes education and training structures—"soft skills" devalue against the ability to interact with AI systems. Companies adopting AI hiring gain short-term speed advantages but risk creating homogeneous teams devoid of creative diversity.

THE ATLANTIC

Labor Crisis • US Politics • Public Health
The analysis goes beyond the usual fear of unemployment. The real threat is the "cognitive obsolescence" of white-collar workers and the compression of the middle class, which was the pillar of democratic institutions. Technology doesn't just replace routine; it devalues expert knowledge, making experience and education less significant. This leads to societal polarization between capital owners (AI systems) and the precariat. Politically, this is fertile ground for a new type of populism. For business, short-term productivity gains will turn into a long-term collapse in consumer demand, as algorithms don't buy goods.
The article reveals the ideological underpinnings of the new wave of American conservatism. It is not just politics, but a quasi-religious movement rejecting secularism and liberal Enlightenment values. The influence of such figures on the Vice President and the ruling elite means cultural wars are moving into a phase of institutionalizing religious dogmas. For business, this is a risk of facing irrational regulation based on moral rather than economic principles. Investors in media, education, and healthcare sectors should prepare for ideological pressure and censorship.
The transformation of higher education under the aegis of major foundations leads to the total politicization of science. Instead of academic freedom, a rigid "social justice" ideological agenda is implemented. This reduces human capital quality: elite university graduates become ideologues, not analysts. For corporations, this creates a competence crisis: diplomas from prestigious universities cease to be markers of professional fitness. In the long run, this undermines American "soft power" competitiveness and innovation potential.
Trump returns the world to a 19th-century state where there are no permanent alliances, only situational interests. This is not just isolationism, but the active destruction of the system the US built over 80 years. Abdicating the role of global arbiter unties the hands of regional aggressors. For global markets, this means the end of the low geopolitical premium era. Risk insurance, logistics, investment protection—all become exponentially more expensive. The world becomes less safe and less wealthy, and the US economy loses the dollar's reserve currency advantage as issuer trust is undermined by unpredictability.
The public health crisis is viewed as an indicator of democratic dysfunction. Lower life expectancy and rising chronic diseases are results of social, not medical, failures: inequality, distrust in science, and eroded social solidarity. This has direct economic consequences: labor force contraction and productivity decline. For investors, the US healthcare sector looks like a toxic bubble absorbing resources without improving outcomes. This is a structural risk for the entire economy that cannot be mitigated by monetary methods.

NEW INTERNATIONALIST

Global South • Logistics • Latin America
Behind the facade of "autonomous" AI lies the exploitation of cheap labor in Global South countries (Kenya, Philippines). Millions label data and moderate toxic content for pennies, ensuring tech giants' profits. This creates ESG risks for companies and potential for new types of labor conflicts. The hidden logic is digital neocolonialism, where resources (data) and labor are extracted from poor countries while value added settles in Silicon Valley. Investors should consider growing reputational risks and potential regulatory intervention in data supply chains.
The UAE is building a logistics empire, buying ports and infrastructure from Africa to Asia. It's not just business, but a geopolitical lever to control world trade. The Emirates are becoming an indispensable intermediary capable of influencing goods flows and bypassing sanctions. For the West, this is a challenge: key supply nodes end up in the hands of a player with their own ambitions. For investors, this signals a closer look at logistics projects in the Indian Ocean and Africa regions, where the UAE acts aggressively and effectively, often displacing traditional players.
US policy in the region shifts from diplomacy to direct military operations under the pretext of fighting drug trafficking. The real goal is resource control (lithium, oil) and regime change (Venezuela). This is a return to the Monroe Doctrine in its harshest form. For commodity markets, this is an instability factor: supplies can be interrupted at any moment. Companies operating in the region risk getting caught in the crossfire of sanctions, nationalization, or direct military intervention. Sovereignty of regional countries is de facto nullified to please Washington's interests.
Stephen Miller, architect of migration policy, turns deportation into an industrial process. This creates a huge market for private prisons, security firms, and surveillance tech. Cruelty becomes a profitable business model. The hidden motive is using fear of migrants to consolidate the electorate and justify authoritarian governance methods. For the economy, this is a risk of losing cheap labor in agriculture and construction, which will drive inflation. The ethical aspect of the business becomes toxic for any companies linked to government contracts in this sphere.
Using AI in military applications (drones, targeting systems) blurs responsibility for killings. Decisions are made by algorithms, lowering the psychological threshold for using force. This leads to endless "automated" conflicts where civilian casualties are written off as "code errors." The military-industrial complex gets the ideal product: a war that requires constant software and hardware updates. For the world, this is a threat of a new arms race where victory goes not to the one with more soldiers, but faster chips. Regulation in this sphere is practically absent.

TECH AI

Human-AI Hybrids • Open Source • Energy
The new "centaur" labor paradigm (human + AI) creates a class of super-productive workers but deepens the gap with those who haven't mastered the tools. Companies shift from hiring for skills to hiring for AI adaptability. The hidden risk lies in critical business process dependence on third-party proprietary models. This requires a fundamental revision of staff training strategies and intellectual property protection protocols to prevent knowledge leaks.
The rising popularity of self-hosted AI agents like OpenClaw is a direct response to cloud giant dominance. Business seeks to regain data control and avoid vendor lock-in. This opens a new market for local server solutions and open-source integrators. For corporations, this is the only way to ensure privacy, but it requires high internal competencies. This trend poses an existential threat to the business model of API-dependent startups.
Analysis of the trajectory toward AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) shows a focus shift from performance to agency and goal-setting capability. This carries fundamental safety and ethical risks that business currently ignores in the race for profit. Regulators are lagging behind, creating a dangerous window of opportunity for the uncontrolled deployment of autonomous systems capable of unpredictable behavior in critical sectors.
The AI boom is hitting physical limits: acute deficits in energy and cooling. Data centers are becoming the new "nuclear plants" in terms of complexity and strategic importance. This is a massive driver for the green energy and nuclear micro-reactor sectors. For investors, this is a clear signal to invest in infrastructure and hardware, as software growth will stall without solving the energy deficit. Environmental risks are becoming direct financial risks.
Tightening AI regulation in the US and EU creates high entry barriers for new players, effectively protecting the entrenched positions of tech giants able to afford compliance costs. Mandatory disclosure of training data and algorithm audits slow down open-source developments. This leads to market consolidation and reduced innovation dynamism. For business, the cost of AI implementation rises significantly due to legal and compliance expenses.

FRONTLINE

India Strategy • EU Weakness • Data Manipulation
US policy under Trump 2.0 shifts to "strategic impatience" and sphere-of-influence realism. The Venezuela operation shows international law is no longer a constraint. For middle powers (India, Brazil), this is a signal: neutrality no longer guarantees safety. It is necessary to invest in hard power and reduce reliance on the dollar system. The world enters a phase where the strong dictate rules and institutions are paralyzed.
India is forced to adapt to US unpredictability, balancing between rapprochement with the West and preserving strategic autonomy. "Personal chemistry" between leaders ceases to be a guarantee against tariffs and sanctions. Risk for the Indian economy—Trump's protectionism and pressure on trade with Russia. Delhi activates ties with Europe and Asia to hedge risks, understanding Washington's unreliability.
Europe demonstrates total inability to resist American unilateralism, despite public humiliations (Vance's speeches). The transatlantic alliance is cracking, but the EU has no alternative and continues to pay for loyalty. This leads to a loss of Europe's geopolitical subjectivity. For business, this means European markets will follow the US sanctions wake, even to the detriment of their own interests.
Manipulation of macroeconomic statistics (GDP, unemployment) in India and the US becomes a tool for elite power retention. The reality of income stagnation and rising inequality is hidden behind "pretty growth numbers." This destroys feedback loops in democracy and leads to social explosions. For investors, official statistics cease to be a reliable market assessment tool, requiring alternative data sources.
Declining Sikh interest in army service is a symptom of deep regional alienation from the center. The impact of the drug crisis, lack of prospects, and political stigmatization creates a social bomb on the border with Pakistan. This carries risks for India's national security and border state stability. The hidden logic is the failure of integration policy and rising separatist sentiment in the diaspora.
