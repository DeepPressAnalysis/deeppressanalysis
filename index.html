01

THE ALGORITHMIC WARFARE PARADIGM

Modern armed conflicts are undergoing a fundamental, irreversible paradigm shift. The era in which battlefield decisions were made solely based on human intuition, human intelligence, and manual data analysis is giving way to high-tech operations dominated by artificial intelligence (AI) systems, large-scale data collection (Big Data), cloud computing, and the deep integration of the cyber domain with kinetic effects.

An analysis of the intelligence and military operations of the State of Israel, particularly the activities of Unit 8200 of the Military Intelligence Directorate (AMAN), demonstrates a globally unprecedented level of implementing automated decision-making systems into the processes of targeting, mass surveillance, and enemy elimination.

Materials from investigative journalism of unprecedented depth by outlets such as +972 Magazine, Local Call, The Guardian, and the Financial Times, along with independent cyber audits and academic studies, reveal the architecture of a fundamentally new form of warfare. In this architecture, machine learning algorithms do not merely assist humans as AI Decision Support Systems (AI-DSS); they de facto assume key sovereign functions for identifying targets, determining their real-time locations, and calculating mathematically permissible collateral damage among civilians.

The practical application of the Lavender, The Gospel, and Where's Daddy systems during the Gaza Strip operation, alongside the highly sophisticated operation—relying on vulnerabilities in the civilian Internet of Things (IoT)—to eliminate Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in February 2026, illustrate two diametrically opposed poles of military AI application. On one hand, there is mass, statistically justified targeting of an indiscriminate nature leading to colossal destruction; on the other, surgically precise cyber espionage stretched over years at the highest level of state leadership.

02

UNIT 8200 AND THE "HUMAN-MACHINE TEAM"

To grasp the scale of the current technological revolution within the Israeli military, one must trace the historical trajectory of the main actor driving these changes: Unit 8200. Founded in 1952 using primitive surplus American military equipment, the unit was initially named the "2nd Intelligence Service Unit," then Unit 515, and Unit 848. Following a catastrophic intelligence failure during the 1973 Yom Kippur War, when Syrian forces captured a Unit 848 officer and gained access to classified data, the structure was radically reformed, renamed Unit 8200, and received unprecedented funding.

Today, Unit 8200 is recognized as one of the most powerful technical intelligence agencies in the world, comparable in capabilities to the US National Security Agency (NSA) and the British Government Communications Headquarters (GCHQ). The unit's personnel, consisting primarily of conscripts aged 18–21 selected through a rigorous school screening system, manage highly complex cyber operations, including the legendary breach of Kaspersky Lab networks, which allowed the Israelis to monitor the activities of Russian government hackers in real time.

The Ideology of General Yossi Sariel

Brigadier General Yossi Sariel, who commanded Unit 8200 from February 2021 to September 2024, became the intellectual and operational architect of the IDF's transition to algorithmic warfare. Sariel radically restructured the unit, shifting the focus from classic linguistic analysis and traditional wiretapping to data engineering and AI development. By October 2023, more than 60% of the unit's staff were data scientists and engineers.

In 2021, Sariel, using the pseudonym "Brigadier General Y.S.," published a book titled The Human-Machine Team, which became a genuine manifesto for Israel's new military doctrine. The book promoted a radical concept: the future of intelligence lies not in the competition between human and algorithm, but in their synergy aimed at achieving a state of "super cognition." Sariel argued that traditional intelligence methods were hopelessly outdated amidst information overload. He called for a massive migration of military databases to secure commercial cloud platforms and the deployment of AI to "generate, prioritize, and strike targets on the battlefield faster than a human adversary can react."

This theoretical foundation soon took the shape of so-called "artificial intelligence factories" deployed at a new targeting center at the Nevatim Airbase. What was described in the book as a futuristic concept became a chilling reality during the operation in the Gaza Strip.

03

MASS TARGETING ARCHITECTURE IN GAZA

Following the events of October 7, 2023, the Israel Defense Forces initiated an unprecedentedly intense bombing campaign in Gaza. While in previous conflicts the IDF regularly faced a "target shortage," the implementation of AI systems transformed the targeting process into an assembly line where the focus shifted from the depth of intelligence verification to mathematical quantity.

An interconnected ecosystem of Israeli military AI functioned on the basis of several key platforms, each performing a specific task within the overall kill chain:

  • Lavender: Identifies and tags individual human targets (combatants). It utilizes machine learning based on cellular metadata, number changes, social media connections, visual data, and WhatsApp group affiliations. The result is assigning every resident a rating (1 to 100) indicating the probability of a Hamas connection, generating a database of 37,000 potential targets.
  • The Gospel (Habsora): Determines structural targets, buildings, and civilian infrastructure. Uses pattern matching of satellite imagery, UAV data, cyber intelligence, and OSINT. Results in automated demolition recommendations and the expansion of targeting parameters to "power targets" (high-rises, banks).
  • Where's Daddy?: Conducts real-time geolocation tracking of individual targets. It synthesizes Lavender data with mobile device geolocation and sensor signals, sending push notifications to operators the moment a suspected militant crosses the threshold of their private family residence.
  • The Alchemist: Provides tactical situational awareness for field units by processing real-time sensor data directly from the battlefield to send threat warnings to commanders' tactical tablets.
  • Depth of Wisdom: Maps underground communications using seismic sensors, geolocation anomalies, and visual intelligence to create 3D models and identify enemy tunnel entrances beneath urban developments.
04

PROJECT LAVENDER AND THE GOSPEL

Statistical Modeling and the Erosion of Humanity

The central element of the new Israeli war machine was the Lavender system. Its algorithm was trained on the data of known Hamas and Palestinian Islamic Jihad (PIJ) militants. These characteristics were fed into the machine as training data, after which the algorithm began searching for similar "features" among the entire population of Gaza, comprising 2.3 million people.

An analysis of Lavender's mechanics reveals profound technical and ethical flaws. Primarily, the algorithm operates on correlations, not causal relationships. For instance, frequently changing mobile phones, moving residences, or accidentally ending up in a shared WhatsApp group with a confirmed militant were assessed by the machine as predictive indicators of terrorist activity. Amidst intense combat and mass displacement, such behavioral patterns were characteristic of virtually the entire civilian population.

According to testimonies from six Israeli intelligence officers, Lavender's accuracy was only 90%. Military command acknowledged and institutionalized this 10% margin of error. When compiling a list of 37,000 potential targets for elimination, this mathematically meant the IDF knowingly authorized the inclusion of at least 3,700 entirely innocent civilians into "kill lists," tagged by the algorithm as false positives.

Furthermore, the much-touted "human-machine team" doctrine practically resulted in the complete elimination of meaningful human oversight. Officers acted as a bureaucratic "rubber stamp," spending an average of about 20 seconds analyzing a target before authorizing a strike. Thus, delegating lethal decisions to an AI black box became a routine practice.

The Gospel and the Doctrine of Domicide

While Lavender generated human targets, The Gospel functioned as an automated tool for infrastructural violence. The IDF Targeting Directorate used pattern-matching algorithms to detect anomalies in satellite imagery and drone video. If intelligence previously generated around 50 targets a year, AI allowed the production of 100 targets a day, with 50% immediately attacked.

A major strategic shift was the use of The Gospel to select "power targets," justifying the destruction of objects lacking a clear military purpose: residential high-rises, universities, and UN infrastructure. Israeli officers admitted in anonymous interviews that destroying civilian infrastructure was designed to create a "shock effect" among the population, break its morale, and force civil society to exert political pressure on Hamas.

05

CALCULATING COLLATERAL DAMAGE & THE COLLAPSE OF IHL

Where's Daddy?

The most cynical component of the system was a program tellingly named Where's Daddy?. The algorithm continuously tracked individuals from the Lavender lists, programmed to send aviation push notifications at the exact moment a suspect crossed the threshold of their private home—usually late at night when their entire family was inside. The IDF deliberately used unguided 2,000-pound "dumb bombs" to destroy the homes of low-level suspects to save expensive precision-guided munitions. Using unguided aerial bombs in hyper-dense urban environments guaranteed mass casualties.

To legitimize this, Israeli command institutionalized the concept of pre-approved civilian kill quotas—so-called "permissible collateral damage." Documentary evidence confirms that military command permitted the killing of 15 to 20 civilians for the elimination of a single junior Hamas member identified by the machine. If marked as a senior commander, the threshold officially increased to over 100 people per strike.

The Collapse of International Humanitarian Law

The use of AI systems in Gaza triggered a profound crisis in interpreting International Humanitarian Law (IHL). Fundamental principles—distinction between combatants and non-combatants, and proportionality—proved incompatible with the algorithmic logic of systems prioritizing speed and scale. The legal community faced a philosophical abyss: how to classify war crimes committed by a machine?

Artificial intelligence is a "black box" yielding non-reproducible results based on hidden correlations. This opacity destroys the concept of command responsibility. When an officer approves a target in 20 seconds, conducting an adequate legal analysis of a strike's proportionality is physically impossible. This creates an "accountability deficit," shifting moral and legal blame from military leadership to the mathematical probability of a machine.

The operational reality of these systems was primarily exposed through independent investigative journalism (by +972 Magazine and Local Call), which faced severe state pressure, with military censorship of the press doubling between 2023 and 2024.

06

OPERATION IN TEHRAN: IOT INFILTRATION

While AI technologies in the Gaza Strip were applied for mass, statistical violence with high institutional tolerance for errors, Israeli intelligence operations against Iran demonstrate a diametrically opposed approach. In Tehran, the same basic algorithms of machine learning, computer vision, and big data processing were used for the surgically precise tracking and elimination of one specific high-ranking figure.

The assassination of Iran's Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei on February 28, 2026, became the absolute culmination of the multi-year integration of cyber operations (coordinated by Unit 8200), human intelligence (Mossad and the CIA), and predictive AI analysis. The foundation of this operation was the hacking and long-term exploitation of almost all traffic surveillance cameras in Iran's capital. Hacked civilian IP cameras belonging to the Iranian state intercepted video streams, securely encrypted them, and transmitted them to servers in Israel.

Therein lies a profound historical irony. A colossal video surveillance network built by an authoritarian government to monitor its own citizens was turned by a foreign adversary into a powerful tool to eliminate the architects of that very regime. These devices created a perfect, zero-cost passive Intelligence, Surveillance, and Reconnaissance (ISR) architecture.

AI Analysis of the "Pattern of Life"

Unit 8200's artificial intelligence analyzed billions of data points to create ultra-detailed "pattern of life" profiles. The 86-year-old Khamenei himself left no digital footprint. Instead, algorithms meticulously studied his surrounding security apparatus: the arrival times of bodyguards, daily travel routes, and exact parking locations. By analyzing the guard routine on Pasteur Street, algorithms learned to predict the Supreme Leader's arrival with mathematical precision.

Minutes before the strike, Israel and the US launched a localized cyberattack: components of cellular base stations in the target area were disabled and jammed. All phones registered a "busy" signal, completely paralyzing Iranian security communications. Following this, IDF aircraft released 30 precision-guided munitions, killing the Supreme Leader and Iran's top military command. This became the benchmark proof of the "sensor-to-shooter timeline" reduction concept.

07

IRANIAN CYBER RETALIATION & THE REMEDIATION GAP

The hacking of Tehran's traffic cameras acutely exposed a global cybersecurity crisis, which experts call the "remediation gap" of civilian cameras. In response to Israeli operations, Iran-linked hacker groups launched a massive campaign to exploit IP cameras across the Middle East (Israel, Qatar, Bahrain, Kuwait, the UAE, Cyprus, and Lebanon).

Iranian intelligence agencies hacked cameras in direct coordination with military operations. Cybersecurity firm Check Point identified three main objectives for utilizing vulnerable devices:

  • Preliminary Reconnaissance: Selecting precise coordinates and approach paths for missile strikes.
  • Battle Damage Assessment (BDA): Obtaining real-time visual feedback on ballistic missile accuracy before official news or satellite imagery emerged.
  • Target Correction: Using live broadcasts to redirect subsequent strikes in the event of a miss.

The root of the problem lies in the structural failure of the IoT market. While agencies like CISA mandate the patching of these vulnerabilities (such as critical RCE flaws in Chinese-manufactured Hikvision and Dahua cameras with CVSS scores of 9.8), updating camera firmware in the corporate sector is not treated with the urgency of server patching. Millions of cameras have direct internet access without network segmentation, leaving a gaping security hole open for years.

08

SURVEILLANCE INTERMEDIARIES: THE AZURE CRISIS

Machine learning, computer vision algorithms, and systems generating tens of thousands of targets require colossal computational power. Unit 8200 physically lacked the internal server infrastructure to store the data required. The solution was turning to "hyperscalers"—the largest providers of commercial cloud computing, notably Microsoft.

By July 2025, Microsoft Azure data centers in the Netherlands and Ireland housed over 11,500 terabytes (13.6 petabytes) of classified Israeli military data. This massive data set included a giant mass surveillance system monitoring Palestinians, capable of processing and transcribing a million phone calls per hour. This data served as training data to refine targeting algorithms like Lavender and The Gospel. During the Gaza war, the corporation provided intelligence units with roughly 19,000 hours of technical and engineering support.

Microsoft's involvement illustrates the concept of "Surveillance Intermediaries." Global tech companies do not merely service states; they become active accomplices in national intelligence operations by holding a monopoly on computational infrastructure. However, this monopoly creates vulnerability to internal civil society. An ethical rebellion flared up within Microsoft, with employees protesting complicity in alleged war crimes. Furthermore, hosting the servers in Europe created colossal legal risks.

Unable to withstand the pressure, in the fall of 2025, Microsoft unilaterally blocked Unit 8200's access to some of its cloud services and AI tools. This incident marked a turning point in global security: transnational corporations now possess sufficient power to disconnect a sovereign state from critical military infrastructure during active combat operations.

09

THE COLLAPSE OF TECHNOLOGICAL DETERMINISM

The unprecedented development of AI in the IDF was accompanied by the birth of a dangerous illusion of technological omnipotence. Despite creating incredibly complex neural networks, Unit 8200's military machine suffered a catastrophic defeat on October 7, 2023. The machines failed to predict Hamas's massive invasion for one simple reason: the militants abandoned electronic communications, retreating into the "technological shadows." Israeli signals intelligence (SIGINT), overly reliant on algorithms, ignored the human factor. The obsession with generating terabytes of data led to cognitive blindness.

The most humiliating failure was the personal cybersecurity of Unit 8200 Commander Yossi Sariel. The man heading the "Israeli NSA" exposed his highly classified identity due to a rudimentary error in digital hygiene. Journalists easily linked the electronic version of his book on Amazon to his personal Google account. Sariel resigned in September 2024, symbolizing the end of the era of blind faith that algorithms can completely replace human intellect and strategic foresight in war.

10

EXECUTIVE ASSESSMENT: THE ARCHITECTURE OF FUTURE WARS

A deep analysis of these operations allows for fundamental conclusions regarding the nature of 21st-century warfare:

  1. The Mathematization of Ethics and the Delegation of Responsibility: The deployment of systems like Lavender shows that modern armies are ready to translate moral dilemmas and international law into statistical probabilities. AI serves as a legal "black box," acting as a shield to absolve commanders of responsibility and institutionalize mathematically permissible civilian casualties.
  2. Absolute Vulnerability via IoT Infrastructure: The assassination of Ali Khamenei proved that multi-billion-dollar national security investments can be neutralized by cheap consumer IP cameras. The global "remediation gap" creates a passive video surveillance network for any state possessing the resources to hack it.
  3. The New Sovereignty of Transnational Corporations: The disconnection of Unit 8200 from Azure cloud services is a historic precedent. Nation-states are losing their monopoly on high-tech warfare. "Surveillance Intermediaries" can paralyze elite intelligence units at the push of a button, inevitably sparking a global race to create sovereign "combat clouds."
  4. The Limits of Technological Determinism: AI is entirely powerless to predict an adversary's intentions if they choose to operate outside the digital environment using mid-20th-century tactics.

The era of algorithmic warfare has irrevocably arrived. Its efficiency is staggering, and its consequences are devastating. The future evolution of conflicts will be determined not just by whose neural networks are more accurate, but by whether humanity can maintain legal and moral control over machines before they turn war into a fully autonomous mathematical process.
