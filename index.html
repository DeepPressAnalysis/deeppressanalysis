Financial Times

Global Trade • Geopolitics • Energy
The Trump administration is raising the stakes in the Middle East conflict by issuing a harsh ultimatum to Tehran, threatening to destroy its energy infrastructure. This move is driven less by military necessity following the successful rescue of an American pilot, and more by Washington's desire to stabilize the global energy market. The blockade of the Strait of Hormuz has already led to a critical spike in oil prices, creating inflationary risks for the US economy ahead of the elections. For the markets, the ultimatum serves a dual signal: on one hand, it demonstrates the US readiness to forcefully unblock logistical arteries; on the other, it sharply increases the geopolitical risk premium. Institutional investors are forced to price in the probability of full-scale strikes on Iranian oil refineries, which could potentially remove significant hydrocarbon volumes from the market. In the long term, Washington's tough rhetoric is aimed at coercing China, which relies heavily on Middle Eastern oil, to exert pressure on Tehran. Such brinkmanship diplomacy benefits the American oil and gas sector, whose capitalization is rising amid global deficits. Simultaneously, the risks of an asymmetric response from pro-Iranian proxy forces in the region are escalating, which will require the US to further divert resources from the Indo-Pacific theater. European allies find themselves in the most vulnerable position, lacking mechanisms to influence the escalation while bearing the brunt of the economic costs. Ultimately, the attempt to resolve the crisis through military force without establishing a new security architecture merely perpetuates instability, turning the Middle East into a zone of permanent force majeure for global supply chains.
The rapid growth in energy consumption by data centers for artificial intelligence is forming a new front of social and institutional tension. The technology sector, previously positioned as environmentally neutral, now faces the risks of stringent regulatory pressure due to the overloading of power grids. Comparing AI to fracking highlights a fundamental shift in perception: innovations are beginning to be viewed as a threat to basic infrastructure and the public good. For investors, this signals the imminent end of the era of uncontrolled expansion of computing power and a transition to a phase of resource rationing. The beneficiaries in this configuration are traditional energy companies and base generation operators, whose assets suddenly acquire a strategic premium. At the same time, tech giants are forced to hedge risks through investments in autonomous energy sources, including small nuclear generation, requiring a shift in their capital expenditure profile. The geopolitical aspect of the problem lies in the intensification of global competition for access to cheap electricity, which may provoke the migration of data centers to jurisdictions with less stringent environmental standards. Such arbitrage creates risks of fragmentation in the global AI market and the emergence of sovereign technological enclaves. Institutional resistance on the ground is already transforming into political capital for populist movements demanding protective tariffs against Big Tech. In the medium term, the crystallization of a new regulatory consensus is highly probable, under which the development of artificial intelligence will be strictly quota-based depending on the available regional energy balance.
The escalation in the Middle East and rising energy prices are creating a structural gap between the monetary policies of the US and the European Union. While the American economy, being a net exporter of energy resources, is capable of absorbing the price shock, Europe faces the classic threat of stagflation. The ECB's intention to continue its rate-hiking cycle is driven by the necessity to curb inflationary expectations, even at the risk of provoking a deep recession in the EU's industrial core. This institutional logic reflects the regulator's fear of repeating past mistakes and losing market trust should it capitulate to inflation. For investors, the divergence in interest rates means an inevitable weakening of the euro against the dollar and a flight of capital into more secure American assets. Strategically, such a scenario benefits Washington, as it solidifies US financial hegemony amid global instability. The European corporate sector, conversely, bears dual costs: the rising price of raw materials and the increasing cost of borrowing, which degrades its global competitiveness. A risk of Europe's deindustrialization emerges, where energy-intensive manufacturing will continue relocating to the US and Asian countries. The political consequences of this process include rising social tensions and the strengthening of euroskeptic positions ahead of key electoral cycles. Thus, the forced rigidity of the ECB exposes the fundamental vulnerability of the European economic model, which depends on external energy resources and lacks sovereign levers to stabilize supply.
Internal political tension surrounding the expansion of mobilization in Ukraine is becoming a key factor determining the willingness of Western partners to continue funding the conflict. Institutional donors and primary allies are beginning to view the demographic deficit as a systemic risk that diminishes the return on military and financial resources invested in Kyiv. The logic of creditors is transforming: from unconditional support to a rigorous audit of the Ukrainian state's ability to physically hold the front line. For the Trump administration, this crisis creates a convenient window of opportunity to legitimize demands for a freeze in the conflict without losing political face. The decline in mobilization potential objectively strengthens Moscow's negotiating position, allowing it to play a game of attrition against both Ukraine and Western arsenals. Arms markets are reacting to this shift by reorienting long-term contracts to replenish internal NATO stockpiles and meet the needs of Asian allies. Domestically, attempts to force conscription threaten to undermine social stability and delegitimize the Ukrainian government in the eyes of the economically active population. The European Union, for its part, recognizes the risks of a new wave of migration and the associated social pressure on the infrastructure of host countries. Strategic uncertainty forces European capitals to quietly hedge their risks, preparing contingency plans in the event of a sudden collapse of the line of contact. Ultimately, the manpower shortage is becoming the insurmountable barrier that compels the Western alliance to reconsider the very paradigm of victory, shifting the focus from territorial reclamation to preserving sovereign statehood within truncated borders.
The blockade of shipping in the Strait of Hormuz has triggered a radical redistribution of margins in the global chemical industry value chain. American manufacturers of plastics and precursors have gained an unprecedented strategic advantage, monetizing the unavailability of Middle Eastern raw materials for their Asian and European competitors. Behind this process lies a fundamental reconfiguration of the market: the isolation of an entire region from global logistics allows US suppliers to dictate pricing under conditions of artificial scarcity. Institutional investors regard this trend as long-term, pricing a sustained premium for geopolitical security into the stocks of American chemical giants. European industrial consumers become the primary victims in this scheme, facing critical cost increases for packaging, auto components, and construction materials. For the markets, this signifies an inevitable transfer of inflationary pressure to the end consumer, complicating central banks' efforts to target inflation. Geoeconomically, the situation cements the status of the US as a dominant industrial hub, independent of external supply shocks in the commodities sector. The risks of this configuration lie in the potential destruction of demand: excessive price hikes could trigger accelerated processes of seeking alternative, lower-quality materials. Furthermore, Asian importers will be forced to accelerate supply diversification, reorienting toward Russian or African chemical capacities. Thus, the short-term super-profits of the American corporate sector mask a profound process of fragmentation of the once-unified global commodities market into isolated, competing blocs.
