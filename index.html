Global Trade • European Union • Markets
The return of aggressive Chinese exports to global markets forms a long-term deflationary risk for Western economies. Beijing is deliberately using a price dumping strategy to export its domestic economic crisis and covertly subsidize its industrial sector. For global markets, this means an immediate intensification of pressure on the profit margins of European and American manufacturers. Such dynamics will inevitably provoke a new wave of protectionism and trade barriers from the US and the EU. Transnational corporations will be forced to accelerate the fragmentation of supply chains, relocating production facilities to neutral jurisdictions. Institutional investors have already begun repricing assets in the heavy machinery and green energy sectors, factoring in the risks of strict prohibitive tariffs. The strategic logic of Washington and Brussels will now come down to forming closed economic perimeters. In the medium term, the main beneficiaries will be ASEAN countries acting as transit hubs to bypass sanctions. However, for emerging markets, this shock carries a high risk of debt crises due to a potential drop in commodity prices. Central banks of developed countries will find themselves in a difficult situation, balancing between protecting local markets and fighting imported deflation. The signal to the markets is clear: the era of global free trade is definitively giving way to geo-economic blocs with rigid manual control.
